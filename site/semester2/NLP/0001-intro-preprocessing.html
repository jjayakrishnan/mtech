1. What is NLP?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the field of AI concerned with giving computers the ability to understand, generate, and manipulate human language.

Two Core Capabilities

  • Natural Language Understanding (NLU): Taking text/speech as input and extracting meaning (parsing, sentiment analysis, question answering)
  • Natural Language Generation (NLG): Producing fluent text/speech as output (machine translation, summarization, dialogue systems)

Why is NLP Hard?

Human language is ambiguous at every level. Unlike programming languages, natural language is full of context-dependent meanings, implicit knowledge, and structural flexibility. A single sentence can have multiple valid interpretations depending on lexical choices, grammatical structure, meaning, and social context.

2. Levels of Linguistic Analysis & Ambiguity

LevelWhat it analyzesType of Ambiguity
LexicalWords and morphemesWord sense (polysemy)

SemanticMeaning of sentencesLogical interpretation
PragmaticContext and intentSpeaker's actual meaning

Classic Example: "I made her duck"

This sentence has at least 5 interpretations:

  1. I cooked a duck for her (made = cooked, duck = noun/bird)
  2. I caused her to duck/bend down (made = caused, duck = verb)
  3. I created a toy duck for her (made = crafted, duck = noun/toy)
  4. I made the duck that belongs to her (her = possessive)
  5. I turned her into a duck (made = transformed)

Lexical ambiguity: "duck" = bird OR the verb to bend. Syntactic ambiguity: ditransitive structure (made [her] [duck]) vs. causative (made [her duck]).

"Colorless green ideas sleep furiously"

Chomsky's famous example. This sentence is syntactically valid (adjective + adjective + noun + verb + adverb) but semantically nonsensical. It demonstrates that grammar and meaning are independent: you can have perfect structure with zero coherent meaning.

"Can you pass the salt?"

Pragmatic ambiguity: Literally, this is a yes/no question about ability. Pragmatically, it is a polite request (an indirect speech act). Understanding the speaker's intent requires world knowledge and social conventions beyond the sentence itself.

3. NLP Preprocessing Pipeline

Raw text is messy. Before any model can work with it, we clean and normalize it through a standard pipeline:

Pipeline Order

Raw Text → Tokenization → Stop Word Removal → Lemmatization / Stemming

Step 1: Tokenization

Splitting text into individual tokens (words, punctuation, numbers). Challenges include abbreviations (Ph.D.), contractions (aren't → are + n't), hyphenated words (AI-Conference), and multi-word expressions.

Step 2: Stop Word Removal

Removing high-frequency words that carry little semantic content: "the", "is", "to", "a", "an", "in", etc. This reduces dimensionality but can lose meaning in some contexts ("to be or not to be").

Step 3: Lemmatization vs. Stemming

StemmingLemmatization
Crude affix stripping (rule-based)Dictionary lookup for base form
Fast, may produce non-wordsSlower, always produces real words
"running" → "run", "studies" → "studi""running" → "run", "studies" → "study"
Porter Stemmer, SnowballWordNet Lemmatizer, spaCy

Worked Example

Input: "Ph.D. students aren't going to the AI-Conference!!"

Step 1 - Tokenization:
["Ph.D.", "students", "are", "n't", "going", "to", "the", "AI-Conference", "!", "!"]

Step 2 - Stop Word Removal: (remove "are", "n't", "to", "the")
["Ph.D.", "students", "going", "AI-Conference"]

Step 3 - Lemmatization:
["Ph.D.", "student", "go", "AI-Conference"]

4. Evaluation Metrics for NLP

Most NLP tasks are evaluated as classification problems. The confusion matrix gives us four counts:

Predicted PositivePredicted Negative
Actual PositiveTP (True Positive)FN (False Negative)
Actual NegativeFP (False Positive)TN (True Negative)

Key Formulas (Closed Book)

\[\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}\]

\[\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}\]

\[\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}\]

\[F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot P \cdot R}{P + R} = \frac{2 \cdot TP}{2 \cdot TP + FP + FN}\]

Intuition: Precision answers "Of everything I flagged as positive, how much was correct?" Recall answers "Of all actual positives, how many did I find?" F1 is the harmonic mean balancing both.

Worked Example

Given: TP = 80, FP = 20, FN = 10, TN = 90

\[\text{Precision} = \frac{80}{80+20} = \frac{80}{100} = 0.80\]

\[\text{Recall} = \frac{80}{80+10} = \frac{80}{90} \approx 0.889\]

\[\text{Accuracy} = \frac{80+90}{80+90+20+10} = \frac{170}{200} = 0.85\]

\[F_1 = \frac{2 \times 0.80 \times 0.889}{0.80 + 0.889} = \frac{1.422}{1.689} \approx 0.842\]

5. Key NLP Applications

  • Machine Translation (MT): Converting text from one language to another (Google Translate, DeepL)
  • Question Answering (QA): Finding answers to natural language questions from a knowledge source
  • Sentiment Analysis: Determining the emotional tone/opinion in text (positive, negative, neutral)
  • Text Summarization: Producing concise summaries of longer documents (extractive vs. abstractive)
  • Named Entity Recognition (NER): Identifying entities (person, location, organization) in text
  • Information Extraction: Extracting structured data from unstructured text

Practice Questions

Question: Apply the full preprocessing pipeline to the sentence:
"The researchers' findings weren't published in Nature-2024."

Show the output after each step: Tokenization, Stop Word Removal, Lemmatization.

Solution:

Tokenization: ["The", "researchers", "'", "findings", "were", "n't", "published", "in", "Nature-2024", "."]

Stop Word Removal: (remove "The", "'", "were", "n't", "in", ".") → ["researchers", "findings", "published", "Nature-2024"]

Lemmatization: ["researcher", "finding", "publish", "Nature-2024"]

Question: A spam classifier has the following results on 500 emails:
- Correctly identified 120 spam emails (TP=120)
- Incorrectly flagged 30 legitimate emails as spam (FP=30)
- Missed 20 spam emails (FN=20)
- Correctly passed 330 legitimate emails (TN=330)

Compute Precision, Recall, Accuracy, and F1.

Solution:

\[\text{Precision} = \frac{120}{120+30} = \frac{120}{150} = 0.80\]

\[\text{Recall} = \frac{120}{120+20} = \frac{120}{140} \approx 0.857\]

\[\text{Accuracy} = \frac{120+330}{500} = \frac{450}{500} = 0.90\]

\[F_1 = \frac{2 \times 0.80 \times 0.857}{0.80 + 0.857} = \frac{1.371}{1.657} \approx 0.827\]

Question: Identify the type(s) of ambiguity in each sentence:

  1. "Time flies like an arrow; fruit flies like a banana."
  2. "The chicken is ready to eat."
  3. "It's cold in here." (said while looking at someone near the window)

Solution:

  1. Lexical + Syntactic: "flies" = verb (to fly) or noun (insects); "like" = preposition (similar to) or verb (enjoy). The two clauses have identical surface structure but completely different parses.
  2. Syntactic: The chicken is ready to eat [something] vs. the chicken is ready [for someone] to eat [it]. PP-attachment ambiguity.
  3. Pragmatic: Literally a statement about temperature. Pragmatically, an indirect request to close the window.
