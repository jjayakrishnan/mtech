1. What is a Language Model?

A Language Model (LM) is a probabilistic machine that does two things:

  1. Predicts the next word: Given a history of words, assign a probability to each possible next word: \(P(w_{\text{next}} | w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{n-1})\)
  2. Scores entire sentences: Assign a probability to a complete word sequence: \(P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n)\)
Why do we need this? Language models are the backbone of: speech recognition (picking the most likely transcription), machine translation (choosing fluent output), autocomplete, spelling correction, and text generation.

2. The Chain Rule of Probability

To compute the probability of a full sentence, we decompose it using the chain rule:

Chain Rule

\[P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \mid w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{i-1})\]

For example:

\[P(\text{"the cat sat"}) = P(\text{the}) \cdot P(\text{cat} \mid \text{the}) \cdot P(\text{sat} \mid \text{the cat})\]

3. The Sparsity Crisis & Markov Assumption

The Problem: To compute \(P(w_n | w_1, \ldots, w_{n-1})\) exactly, we need counts for every possible prefix. With a vocabulary of 50,000 words, a 10-word history has \(50000^{10}\) possible contexts. We can never observe enough data.

Markov Assumption

Approximate by looking at only the last N-1 words instead of the full history:

\[P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-N+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})\]

This is the N-gram assumption: the future depends only on the recent past.

Trade-off: Larger N captures more context (better predictions) but needs exponentially more data to estimate reliably (more sparsity).

5. MLE Estimation

Maximum Likelihood Estimation: Count occurrences in a training corpus and normalize.

Bigram MLE

\[P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{C(w_{i-1})}\]

where \(C(w_{i-1}, w_i)\) is the count of the bigram and \(C(w_{i-1})\) is the count of the context word.

Worked Example

Corpus (3 sentences):

  • "a b a b a"
  • "a b c"
  • "b a c"

Compute P(b | a):

Count all bigrams starting with "a":

  • Sentence 1: (a,b), (b,a), (a,b), (b,a), so bigrams with "a" as first: (a,b), (a,b) → C(a,b)=2 from this sentence, C(a)=2
  • Sentence 2: (a,b), (b,c) → C(a,b)=1, C(a)=1
  • Sentence 3: (b,a), (a,c) → C(a,c)=1, C(a)=1

Totals: C(a,b) = 3, C(a,c) = 1, C(a) appears as first in bigram = there are various counts. Let us simplify:

Looking at all tokens: Count(a) = 5 times "a" appears as a non-final context. Count(a,b) = 3 bigrams "ab" total (from sentence 1: 2 + sentence 2: 1). But let us take a clean example:

Simpler corpus: "a b a b a b c a c" (single stream)

Bigrams: (a,b)(b,a)(a,b)(b,a)(a,b)(b,c)(c,a)(a,c)

C(a) as context = 5 (appears 5 times followed by something): positions 1,3,5,8 ... let's count: a appears at positions 1,3,5,8 as first element of bigram. So C(a)=4.

Cleanest example:

Corpus: "a b a b a c b a b c" → Bigrams: (a,b)(b,a)(a,b)(b,a)(a,c)(c,b)(b,a)(a,b)(b,c)

C(a,b) = 3, C(a,c) = 1, C(a) as context = 4

\[P(b \mid a) = \frac{C(a,b)}{C(a)} = \frac{3}{4} = 0.75\]

6. The Zero Problem

If a bigram never appeared in the training corpus, MLE gives it probability zero:

\[C(w_{i-1}, w_i) = 0 \implies P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = 0\]

Why this is catastrophic: Since sentence probability is a product of word probabilities, a single zero term makes the entire sentence probability zero. A perfectly valid sentence gets rated as "impossible" just because one word pair was missing from training data.

7. Smoothing: Add-1 (Laplace)

The simplest fix: add 1 to every count, pretending we saw each possible bigram at least once.

Add-1 (Laplace) Smoothing

\[P^*(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}, w_i) + 1}{C(w_{i-1}) + V}\]

where \(V\) = vocabulary size (number of unique word types).

Intuition: We add 1 to the numerator (every bigram gets at least 1 count). To keep it a valid probability distribution, we add V to the denominator (since there are V possible next words, each getting +1).

Worked Example

Given: C(a,b) = 2, C(a) = 5, V = 4 (vocabulary has 4 unique words)

\[P^*(b \mid a) = \frac{C(a,b) + 1}{C(a) + V} = \frac{2 + 1}{5 + 4} = \frac{3}{9} = 0.333\]

Compare without smoothing: \(P(b|a) = 2/5 = 0.4\). Smoothing "steals" probability mass from seen events and redistributes it to unseen ones.

8. Interpolation

Mix different order N-grams to get the best of all worlds: trigrams have context but are sparse; unigrams are robust but context-free.

Linear Interpolation

\[\hat{P}(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) = \lambda_1 \cdot P_{\text{tri}}(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}) + \lambda_2 \cdot P_{\text{bi}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) + \lambda_3 \cdot P_{\text{uni}}(w_i)\]

where \(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 1\) and all \(\lambda_i \geq 0\).

Worked Example

Given: \(\lambda_1 = 0.5, \lambda_2 = 0.3, \lambda_3 = 0.2\)

\(P_{\text{tri}} = 0.2, \quad P_{\text{bi}} = 0.05, \quad P_{\text{uni}} = 0.01\)

\[\hat{P} = 0.5 \times 0.2 + 0.3 \times 0.05 + 0.2 \times 0.01\]

\[= 0.10 + 0.015 + 0.002 = 0.117\]

Even if the trigram count were zero, the bigram and unigram components ensure a non-zero probability.

9. Perplexity

Perplexity is the standard intrinsic evaluation metric for language models. It measures how "surprised" the model is by test data.

Perplexity

\[PP(S) = P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_N)^{-1/N} = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{N} P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})\right)^{-1/N}\]

Lower perplexity = better model.

Intuition: Perplexity is the weighted average branching factor. A perplexity of 100 means the model is as confused as if it had to choose uniformly among 100 words at each step.

Worked Example

Sentence: \(w_1 w_2 w_3\) with N = 3

Given probabilities: \(P(w_1) = 0.5, \quad P(w_2 \mid w_1) = 0.2, \quad P(w_3 \mid w_1, w_2) = 0.1\)

\[P(S) = 0.5 \times 0.2 \times 0.1 = 0.01\]

\[PP(S) = (0.01)^{-1/3} = \left(\frac{1}{0.01}\right)^{1/3} = 100^{1/3} \approx 4.64\]

The model's perplexity on this sentence is about 4.64 (as if choosing among ~5 equally likely words at each position).

10. Working in Log Space

Multiplying many small probabilities causes arithmetic underflow (numbers too small for floating point). Solution: work in log space.

\[\log P(w_1, \ldots, w_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log P(w_i \mid w_{i-N+1}, \ldots, w_{i-1})\]

Products become sums. Comparisons are preserved (log is monotonic). Perplexity in log space:

\[\log PP(S) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 P(w_i \mid \text{context})\]

Practice Questions

Question: Given this corpus:

<s> I am Sam </s>
<s> Sam I am </s>
<s> I am happy </s>

Compute: (a) P(am | I), (b) P(Sam | am), (c) P(</s> | Sam)

Solution:

Count bigrams:

  • C(I, am) = 3 (appears in all three sentences)
  • C(I) = 3 (appears 3 times as context: once per sentence)
  • C(am, Sam) = 1 (sentence 1)
  • C(am) = 3 (once per sentence)
  • C(Sam, </s>) = 1 (sentence 1)
  • C(Sam) = 2 (sentence 1 "Sam </s>" and sentence 2 "Sam I")

(a) \(P(\text{am} | \text{I}) = \frac{3}{3} = 1.0\)

(b) \(P(\text{Sam} | \text{am}) = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333\)

(c) \(P(\langle/s\rangle | \text{Sam}) = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5\)

Question: Using the same corpus above, vocabulary V = {I, am, Sam, happy, <s>, </s>} so |V| = 6.

Compute the smoothed probability: P*(happy | Sam)

Note: C(Sam, happy) = 0 (never seen!)

Solution:

\[P^*(\text{happy} | \text{Sam}) = \frac{C(\text{Sam}, \text{happy}) + 1}{C(\text{Sam}) + V} = \frac{0 + 1}{2 + 6} = \frac{1}{8} = 0.125\]

Without smoothing this would be 0. With Add-1, it gets a small but non-zero probability.

Question: A bigram model gives these probabilities for the sentence "I am happy":

  • P(I | <s>) = 0.6
  • P(am | I) = 1.0
  • P(happy | am) = 0.2
  • P(</s> | happy) = 0.8

Compute the perplexity. (Use N = 4 including </s> but not <s>)

Solution:

\[P(S) = 0.6 \times 1.0 \times 0.2 \times 0.8 = 0.096\]

\[PP(S) = (0.096)^{-1/4} = \left(\frac{1}{0.096}\right)^{0.25} = (10.417)^{0.25} \approx 1.80\]

Very low perplexity! The model is quite confident about this sentence.

Question: Given \(\lambda_1 = 0.6, \lambda_2 = 0.3, \lambda_3 = 0.1\) and:

  • \(P_{\text{tri}}(\text{happy} | \text{I am}) = 0.0\) (unseen trigram!)
  • \(P_{\text{bi}}(\text{happy} | \text{am}) = 0.2\)
  • \(P_{\text{uni}}(\text{happy}) = 0.05\)

Compute the interpolated probability.

Solution:

\[\hat{P} = 0.6 \times 0.0 + 0.3 \times 0.2 + 0.1 \times 0.05\]

\[= 0 + 0.06 + 0.005 = 0.065\]

Even though the trigram probability is zero, interpolation gives a non-zero result by backing off to bigram and unigram estimates.
