1. The Problem of POS Ambiguity

Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging assigns a grammatical category to each word in a sentence. The challenge: many words are ambiguous.

Classic Example

"I can can the can."
• I/PRP • can/MD (modal verb) • can/VB (to preserve) • the/DT • can/NN (container)
Same spelling, three different parts of speech!

More examples of ambiguous words:

  • "book" — NN (a book) vs. VB (to book a flight)
  • "run" — VB (to run) vs. NN (a run in baseball)
  • "back" — RB (go back), JJ (back door), NN (my back), VB (back the car)

Studies show that roughly 40% of word types in English are ambiguous, and about 60% of word tokens in running text have multiple possible tags.

The Tagging Task

Given a sentence \(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n\), find the best tag sequence \(t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\) such that \(\hat{t}_{1:n} = \arg\max_{t_{1:n}} P(t_{1:n} | w_{1:n})\).

3. Hidden Markov Models for Tagging

An HMM models the tagging problem as a sequence of hidden states (tags) that produce observable outputs (words):

HMM Components

• States \(Q = \{q_1, \ldots, q_N\}\): the POS tags (hidden — what we want to find)
• Observations \(O = \{o_1, \ldots, o_T\}\): the words (visible — what we see)
• Transition matrix \(\mathbf{A}\): \(a_{ij} = P(t_j | t_i)\) — probability of moving from tag \(i\) to tag \(j\)
• Emission matrix \(\mathbf{B}\): \(b_j(o_t) = P(w_t | t_j)\) — probability that tag \(j\) produces word \(w_t\)
• Initial probabilities \(\boldsymbol{\pi}\): \(\pi_i = P(t_1 = i)\) — probability of starting with tag \(i\)

The Two Key Assumptions

  1. Markov assumption: The current tag depends only on the previous tag: \(P(t_i | t_1, \ldots, t_{i-1}) = P(t_i | t_{i-1})\)
  2. Output independence: The current word depends only on the current tag: \(P(w_i | t_1, \ldots, t_n, w_1, \ldots, w_n) = P(w_i | t_i)\)

Computing Probabilities from Counts

Transition probability:

\[P(t_i | t_{i-1}) = \frac{C(t_{i-1}, t_i)}{C(t_{i-1})}\]

Emission probability:

\[P(w_i | t_i) = \frac{C(t_i, w_i)}{C(t_i)}\]
Plain English

Transition: "After seeing a determiner (DT), how often do we see a noun (NN) next?" Count DT→NN pairs in training data, divide by total DT occurrences.
Emission: "When the tag is NN, how often is the word 'dog'?" Count (NN, dog) pairs, divide by total NN occurrences.

The Joint Probability

The HMM assigns a joint probability to a tag sequence and word sequence:

\[P(t_{1:n}, w_{1:n}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(t_i | t_{i-1}) \cdot P(w_i | t_i)\]

(with \(t_0\) being a special START symbol)

4. The Ice Cream / Weather Analogy

The classic textbook example that makes HMMs intuitive:

Setup

You are a climatologist in the year 2799. You find an old diary that only records how many ice creams someone ate each day (1, 2, or 3). You want to figure out the weather (Hot or Cold) for each day — but the weather is never recorded directly.

  • Hidden states: Weather = {Hot, Cold}
  • Observations: Ice creams eaten = {1, 2, 3}
  • Transition: P(Hot|Hot) = 0.7, P(Cold|Hot) = 0.3, P(Hot|Cold) = 0.4, P(Cold|Cold) = 0.6
  • Emission: P(1|Hot) = 0.2, P(2|Hot) = 0.4, P(3|Hot) = 0.4; P(1|Cold) = 0.5, P(2|Cold) = 0.4, P(3|Cold) = 0.1

Mapping to POS tagging:

Ice Cream ProblemPOS Tagging
Hidden states = Weather (Hot/Cold)Hidden states = POS tags (NN/VB/DT/...)
Observations = Ice creams (1/2/3)Observations = Words (the/cat/sat/...)
Task: Infer weather sequenceTask: Infer tag sequence
Transition: P(weather today | weather yesterday)Transition: P(tag_i | tag_{i-1})
Emission: P(ice creams | weather)Emission: P(word | tag)

5. The Viterbi Algorithm

Given observations and HMM parameters, find the most likely hidden state sequence. This is the decoding problem.

Why not brute force?

For \(N\) states and \(T\) time steps, there are \(N^T\) possible sequences. With 45 POS tags and a 20-word sentence: \(45^{20} \approx 10^{33}\) sequences. Impossible to enumerate!

Viterbi uses dynamic programming — it builds a trellis (table) left to right, at each step keeping only the best path to each state.

The Recursion

\[v_t(j) = \max_{i=1}^{N} \left[ v_{t-1}(i) \cdot a_{ij} \right] \cdot b_j(o_t)\]

where:

  • \(v_t(j)\) = probability of the best path ending in state \(j\) at time \(t\)
  • \(a_{ij}\) = transition probability from state \(i\) to state \(j\)
  • \(b_j(o_t)\) = emission probability of observation \(o_t\) from state \(j\)

Backpointer

\[\text{bt}_t(j) = \arg\max_{i=1}^{N} \left[ v_{t-1}(i) \cdot a_{ij} \right]\]

Records which previous state gave the best path — used to trace back the optimal sequence at the end.

Algorithm Steps

  1. Initialization (t=1): \(v_1(j) = \pi_j \cdot b_j(o_1)\) for all states \(j\)
  2. Recursion (t=2,...,T): Apply the formula above for each state at each time step
  3. Termination: Best final state = \(\arg\max_j v_T(j)\)
  4. Backtrace: Follow backpointers from the best final state back to t=1
Exam Tip

Complexity: \(O(N^2 \cdot T)\) — for each of T time steps, for each of N states, we check all N previous states. Compare to brute force \(O(N^T)\). This is the power of dynamic programming.

6. Worked Example: Full Viterbi Trace

Setup: Ice cream HMM with 2 states (Hot, Cold), observations = [3, 1, 3]

Parameters:

  • Initial: \(\pi_H = 0.8, \; \pi_C = 0.2\)
  • Transition: \(a_{HH} = 0.7, \; a_{HC} = 0.3, \; a_{CH} = 0.4, \; a_{CC} = 0.6\)
  • Emission: \(b_H(1)=0.2, \; b_H(2)=0.4, \; b_H(3)=0.4\)
          \(b_C(1)=0.5, \; b_C(2)=0.4, \; b_C(3)=0.1\)

Step 1: Initialization (t=1, observe "3")

\(v_1(H) = \pi_H \cdot b_H(3) = 0.8 \times 0.4 = \mathbf{0.32}\)

\(v_1(C) = \pi_C \cdot b_C(3) = 0.2 \times 0.1 = \mathbf{0.02}\)

Step 2: Recursion (t=2, observe "1")

\(v_2(H) = \max\begin{cases} v_1(H) \cdot a_{HH} \\ v_1(C) \cdot a_{CH} \end{cases} \cdot b_H(1)\)

\(= \max(0.32 \times 0.7, \; 0.02 \times 0.4) \times 0.2 = \max(0.224, \; 0.008) \times 0.2 = 0.224 \times 0.2 = \mathbf{0.0448}\)

Backpointer: bt_2(H) = H (came from Hot)

\(v_2(C) = \max\begin{cases} v_1(H) \cdot a_{HC} \\ v_1(C) \cdot a_{CC} \end{cases} \cdot b_C(1)\)

\(= \max(0.32 \times 0.3, \; 0.02 \times 0.6) \times 0.5 = \max(0.096, \; 0.012) \times 0.5 = 0.096 \times 0.5 = \mathbf{0.048}\)

Backpointer: bt_2(C) = H (came from Hot)

Step 3: Recursion (t=3, observe "3")

\(v_3(H) = \max(0.0448 \times 0.7, \; 0.048 \times 0.4) \times 0.4 = \max(0.0314, \; 0.0192) \times 0.4 = 0.0314 \times 0.4 = \mathbf{0.01254}\)

Backpointer: bt_3(H) = H

\(v_3(C) = \max(0.0448 \times 0.3, \; 0.048 \times 0.6) \times 0.1 = \max(0.01344, \; 0.0288) \times 0.1 = 0.0288 \times 0.1 = \mathbf{0.00288}\)

Backpointer: bt_3(C) = C

Step 4: Termination & Backtrace

Best final state: \(\arg\max(v_3(H), v_3(C)) = \arg\max(0.01254, 0.00288) = \mathbf{H}\)

Backtrace: t=3: H ← bt_3(H)=H ← bt_2(H)=H

Viterbi Trellis — each cell shows δ(state, t); arrows show the winning backpointer; highlighted path = optimal sequence:

t=1 (obs: 3)t=2 (obs: 1)t=3 (obs: 3)
Hot0.3200.0448 ← H0.01254 ← H
Cold0.0200.048 ← H0.00288 ← C

Optimal sequence: Hot → Hot → Hot

Interpretation: On days where 3 ice creams were eaten, it was probably hot. The "1 ice cream" day was still likely hot because the transition from Hot→Hot (0.7) is strong.

Common Mistake

Students often forget to multiply by the emission probability at each step. The formula is: (best incoming path probability) TIMES (emission of current observation). Both factors are essential.

7. Maximum Entropy Markov Models (MEMMs)

A discriminative alternative to HMMs:

Key Difference

HMM (generative): models joint \(P(\text{tags}, \text{words})\) = transition × emission
MEMM (discriminative): models conditional \(P(\text{tag}_i | \text{word}_i, \text{tag}_{i-1}, \text{features})\) directly

Why switch from HMMs to MEMMs?

  • Arbitrary features: Can use word shape (capitalized? has suffix -ing? contains digit?), prefix/suffix, neighboring words
  • No independence assumption on observations: HMM says word depends ONLY on its tag; MEMM can condition on any observable features
  • Better accuracy in practice: Because it directly models what we care about — P(tag|evidence)

Feature Examples for POS Tagging

FeatureWhat it captures
Current word = "running"Lexical identity
Suffix = "-ing"Likely VBG (gerund)
Previous tag = DTAfter determiner, expect NN or JJ
Word is capitalizedLikely NNP (proper noun)
Word contains hyphenLikely JJ (adjective like "well-known")
Previous word = "very"Next word likely JJ or RB
Exam Tip

HMM = generative, uses Bayes rule, limited features (only word identity).
MEMM = discriminative, uses logistic regression/MaxEnt, rich features.
If asked "advantage of MEMM over HMM" — answer: ability to use arbitrary, overlapping features.

Label Bias Problem

MEMMs suffer from the "label bias problem": states with fewer outgoing transitions effectively ignore the observation. CRFs (Conditional Random Fields) fix this but are beyond mid-sem scope.

Formula Cards — Memorize for Exam

Card 1: Viterbi Recursion

\[v_t(j) = \max_{i=1}^{N} \left[ v_{t-1}(i) \cdot a_{ij} \right] \cdot b_j(o_t)\]
Mnemonic: "Best-path-to-here = (max over previous states of [their-best-path times transition]) times emission-of-current-obs."

Card 2: HMM Joint Probability

\[P(t_{1:n}, w_{1:n}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(t_i | t_{i-1}) \cdot P(w_i | t_i)\]
Product of all transitions times all emissions. This is what Viterbi maximizes over tag sequences.

Card 3: Transition Probability (from counts)

\[P(t_i | t_{i-1}) = \frac{C(t_{i-1}, t_i)}{C(t_{i-1})}\]
Count bigram tag pairs, divide by unigram count of the conditioning tag. MLE estimate.

Card 4: Emission Probability (from counts)

\[P(w_i | t_i) = \frac{C(t_i, w_i)}{C(t_i)}\]
How often does this word appear with this tag, divided by how often the tag appears total.

Card 5: Viterbi Initialization

\[v_1(j) = \pi_j \cdot b_j(o_1)\]
Start probability times emission of first observation. No transition yet at t=1.

Try It Yourself

Exercise 1: Compute Transition & Emission Probabilities

From a tagged corpus, you count:

  • C(DT) = 1000, C(NN) = 2000, C(VB) = 1500
  • C(DT, NN) = 800, C(DT, VB) = 50, C(DT, JJ) = 150
  • C(NN, "dog") = 120, C(NN, "cat") = 80, C(VB, "run") = 200

Compute: (a) P(NN|DT)   (b) P(VB|DT)   (c) P("dog"|NN)   (d) P("run"|VB)

Solution:

(a) \(P(\text{NN}|\text{DT}) = \frac{C(\text{DT}, \text{NN})}{C(\text{DT})} = \frac{800}{1000} = \mathbf{0.80}\)

(b) \(P(\text{VB}|\text{DT}) = \frac{50}{1000} = \mathbf{0.05}\)

(c) \(P(\text{"dog"}|\text{NN}) = \frac{120}{2000} = \mathbf{0.06}\)

(d) \(P(\text{"run"}|\text{VB}) = \frac{200}{1500} = \mathbf{0.133}\)

Note: P(NN|DT) = 0.80 makes intuitive sense — after "the/a/this" we usually see a noun.

Exercise 2: Mini Viterbi (2 states, 3 observations)

States: {A, B}. Observations: [x, y, x].

Parameters:

  • Initial: \(\pi_A = 0.6, \; \pi_B = 0.4\)
  • Transition: \(a_{AA}=0.7, \; a_{AB}=0.3, \; a_{BA}=0.5, \; a_{BB}=0.5\)
  • Emission: \(b_A(x)=0.8, \; b_A(y)=0.2, \; b_B(x)=0.3, \; b_B(y)=0.7\)

Trace the Viterbi algorithm and find the best state sequence.

Solution:

t=1 (obs: x):

\(v_1(A) = 0.6 \times 0.8 = 0.48\)

\(v_1(B) = 0.4 \times 0.3 = 0.12\)

t=2 (obs: y):

\(v_2(A) = \max(0.48 \times 0.7, \; 0.12 \times 0.5) \times 0.2 = \max(0.336, 0.06) \times 0.2 = 0.0672\) [from A]

\(v_2(B) = \max(0.48 \times 0.3, \; 0.12 \times 0.5) \times 0.7 = \max(0.144, 0.06) \times 0.7 = 0.1008\) [from A]

t=3 (obs: x):

\(v_3(A) = \max(0.0672 \times 0.7, \; 0.1008 \times 0.5) \times 0.8 = \max(0.04704, 0.0504) \times 0.8 = 0.0504 \times 0.8 = 0.04032\) [from B]

\(v_3(B) = \max(0.0672 \times 0.3, \; 0.1008 \times 0.5) \times 0.3 = \max(0.02016, 0.0504) \times 0.3 = 0.0504 \times 0.3 = 0.01512\) [from B]

Termination: Best = A (0.04032 > 0.01512)

Backtrace: t=3: A ← B ← A ← start

Optimal sequence: A → B → A

Makes sense: state A emits x with high probability (0.8), state B emits y with high probability (0.7).

Exercise 3: HMM Joint Probability

Using the POS tagging HMM, compute P(DT NN VB, "the cat sat") given:

  • P(DT|START) = 0.4
  • P(NN|DT) = 0.8, P(VB|NN) = 0.3
  • P("the"|DT) = 0.5, P("cat"|NN) = 0.06, P("sat"|VB) = 0.04

Solution:

\(P(\text{DT NN VB}, \text{"the cat sat"})\)

\(= P(\text{DT}|\text{START}) \cdot P(\text{"the"}|\text{DT}) \cdot P(\text{NN}|\text{DT}) \cdot P(\text{"cat"}|\text{NN}) \cdot P(\text{VB}|\text{NN}) \cdot P(\text{"sat"}|\text{VB})\)

\(= 0.4 \times 0.5 \times 0.8 \times 0.06 \times 0.3 \times 0.04\)

\(= 0.4 \times 0.5 \times 0.8 \times 0.06 \times 0.3 \times 0.04\)

\(= \mathbf{0.0000576}\) (or \(5.76 \times 10^{-5}\))

Note: These probabilities get very small for longer sentences — in practice we use log probabilities to avoid underflow.
