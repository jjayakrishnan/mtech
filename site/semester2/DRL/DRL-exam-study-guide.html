2. Topic Reference

Topic 1

Multi-Armed Bandit

  • No state — just actions + rewards
  • UCB: \( Q(a) + c\sqrt{\ln t / N(a)} \)
  • EWMA: \( Q(a) \leftarrow Q(a) + \alpha[r - Q(a)] \)
  • \(\varepsilon\)-greedy: explore with prob \(\varepsilon\)
  • \(\varepsilon\)-case: random action chosen
  • \(\alpha=1\) → only last reward matters
Very High Frequency
L2: MAB vs MDP L4: Action-Value Methods L5: UCB
Topic 2

MDP Formulation

  • Tuple: \((S, A, P, R, \gamma)\)
  • Bellman: \( V_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a|s)\sum_{s'} P[R + \gamma V(s')] \)
  • Value Iteration: \( V \leftarrow \max_a \) over actions
  • Synchronous VI: update all states at once
  • Episodic vs continuing tasks
  • \(\gamma \approx 0\): myopic; \(\gamma \approx 1\): far-sighted
Very High Frequency
L6: Finite MDP L7: Bellman Equations L3: Value Iteration
Topic 3

Monte Carlo Methods

  • First-visit: only first occurrence in episode
  • \( G = r_1 + \gamma r_2 + \gamma^2 r_3 + \cdots \) (work backwards)
  • \( V(s) \leftarrow V(s) + \tfrac{1}{n}[G - V(s)] \)
  • Q update → greedy policy improvement
  • Off-policy: importance sampling ratio \(\rho\)
  • Weighted IS: \(\rho \cdot G / \sum \rho\)
Very High Frequency
L9: Monte Carlo
Topic 4

Policy Gradient

  • REINFORCE: \( \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha G \nabla \ln \pi(a|s) \)
  • Softmax policy: \( \pi(a|s) = \sigma(\theta^\top \phi(s,a)) \)
  • Baseline: reduces variance (subtract b from G)
  • Without baseline: high variance updates
  • A2C: Actor picks actions; Critic estimates V
  • Advantage: \( A(s,a) = Q(s,a) - V(s) \)
High Frequency
L8: Dynamic Programming
Topic 5

MAB vs MDP Classification

  • MAB: no state transitions, i.i.d. rewards
  • MDP: current action affects future states
  • Key test: does today's action change tomorrow's context?
  • Episodic: clear start/end (e.g., ICU stays)
  • Continuous: no terminal state
Medium Frequency
L2: MAB vs MDP L1: RL Framework
Topic 6

DQN

  • Experience replay: breaks correlation
  • Target network: separate frozen network for TD target
  • Loss: \( (r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q_{\text{target}}(s') - Q(s,a))^2 \)
  • Deadly triad: FA + bootstrapping + off-policy
Medium Frequency
L8: Dynamic Programming
Topic 7

MCTS & AlphaGo/Zero

  • UCB for trees: \( Q(s,a) + c\sqrt{N(s)/N(s,a)} \)
  • 4 phases: Select → Expand → Simulate → Backprop
  • AlphaGo: trained on human games + self-play
  • AlphaZero: self-play only → more general
Medium Frequency
L5: UCB (foundation)
Topic 8

Imitation Learning

  • BC loss: \( \mathcal{L} = -\sum \log \pi_\theta(a^E \mid s) \)
  • Failure: covariate shift (unseen states at test)
  • DAGGER: aggregate expert labels on policy's own states
  • Advantage: policy learns to recover from mistakes
Lower Frequency
L9: Monte Carlo (related)

3. Key Formulas — Cheat Sheet

UCB Action Selection (MAB)
$$ a^* = \arg\max_a \left[ Q(a) + c \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N(a)}} \right] $$ \(t\) = total time steps so far  ·  \(N(a)\) = times \(a\) was chosen
Exponential Recency-Weighted Average (EWMA)
$$ Q_{n+1}(a) = Q_n(a) + \alpha \left[ R_n - Q_n(a) \right] $$ \(\alpha \in (0,1]\)  ·  \(\alpha = 0.5\) common in exams
Bellman Expectation (Policy Evaluation)
$$ V_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a|s) \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)\left[ R(s,a,s') + \gamma V_\pi(s') \right] $$
Bellman Optimality (Value Iteration)
$$ V^*(s) = \max_a \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)\left[ R + \gamma V^*(s') \right] $$
Discounted Return (MC)
$$ G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma R_{t+2} + \gamma^2 R_{t+3} + \cdots $$ Compute backwards from terminal state
First-Visit MC Update
$$ V(s) \leftarrow V(s) + \frac{1}{n}\left[ G - V(s) \right] $$ \(n\) = number of times \(s\) has been visited so far
Importance Sampling Ratio
$$ \rho = \prod_t \frac{\pi(a_t|s_t)}{b(a_t|s_t)} $$ \(\pi\) = target policy  ·  \(b\) = behaviour policy
Policy Gradient Update (REINFORCE)
$$ \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha (G - b)\, \nabla_\theta \ln \pi_\theta(a|s) $$ \(b\) = baseline (often \(V(s)\) or a constant)
Linear Softmax Policy
$$ \pi(a|s) = \frac{\exp(\theta^\top \phi(s,a))}{\sum_{a'} \exp(\theta^\top \phi(s,a'))} $$ \(\phi(s)\) = feature vector of state \(s\)
UCB for MCTS (Tree)
$$ \text{UCB}(s,a) = Q(s,a) + c\sqrt{\frac{\ln N(s)}{N(s,a)}} $$ \(N(s)\) = parent visits  ·  \(N(s,a)\) = child visits
DQN Loss
$$ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q_{\theta^-}(s',a') - Q_\theta(s,a)\right)^2\right] $$ \(\theta^-\) = frozen target network weights
Behavior Cloning Loss
$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \log \pi_\theta(a_i^E \mid s_i) $$ \(a^E\) = expert action at state \(s\)

(d) [1 Mark] What happens if baseline b is not used? Explain w.r.t. the AI tutor only.

Without b, every positive return (G > 0) reinforces the chosen action — even marginally positive ones. The tutor could lock onto Hard tasks that gave small positive returns, ignoring that Medium tasks might give much higher returns. This high-variance updating destabilises the policy.

(d-1) [1.5 Marks] Write the behavior cloning loss function for policy π_θ(a|s). State one major failure mode when the policy encounters unseen states.

Question 1[1.5 + 2 + 4 = 7.5 Marks]
A mobile health-monitoring wristband must choose among three modes each hour based on current activity {resting, moderate, high}: Mode A (low power, moderate accuracy), Mode B (medium power, high accuracy), Mode C (high power, very high accuracy). Immediate reward from each mode changes with physical activity.
(a) State why RL (not supervised learning) fits this setting. Give a one-line distinction between immediate reward and long-term value. [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (a)

Why RL: No labelled dataset of (activity → correct mode) exists; the agent must discover through interaction that some mode choices yield better cumulative health outcomes than others — a trial-and-error problem with delayed feedback.

Immediate reward vs long-term value: Immediate reward \(R_t\) is the scalar feedback at step \(t\); long-term value \(V_\pi(s)\) is the expected discounted sum of future rewards \(\mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k R_{t+k}\mid s_t{=}s\right]\).

(b) Case 1: user activity is independent of the modes chosen (no state transitions). Case 2: if the wristband samples in Mode C too long, battery temperature rises, reducing future rewards for power-hungry modes. Classify each as MAB or Finite MDP with one justification. [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (b)

Case 1 → Multi-Armed Bandit. Today's mode choice does not affect future activity patterns; rewards are i.i.d. per action. No state transitions → MAB.

Case 2 → Finite MDP. Choosing Mode C changes battery temperature, which alters future reward structure. Current action affects the next state → state transitions exist → MDP.

Question 2[2.5 + 2 + 1.5 + 1.5 = 7.5 Marks]
ICU ventilator AI. States: {lowO2, highO2, OptimalO2}. Actions: {increase_pressure, decrease_pressure, maintain}. Maintaining always retains current state (P=1.0). Increasing/decreasing pressure changes to target state with P=0.6, stays with P=0.4. Goal: stabilise SpO₂. Terminal: 4 consecutive OptimalO2 recordings.
(a) Formulate the MDP (state, action, transition, reward, terminal). [2.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (a)

States S: {lowO2, highO2, OptimalO2, Stabilized}. Stabilized = terminal (absorbing) state after 4 consecutive OptimalO2.

Actions A: {increase_pressure, decrease_pressure, maintain}

Transitions P(s'|s,a):

  • maintain → P(same state) = 1.0 from any non-terminal state
  • increase from lowO2 → P(OptimalO2)=0.6, P(lowO2)=0.4
  • increase from OptimalO2 → P(highO2)=0.6, P(OptimalO2)=0.4
  • decrease from highO2 → P(OptimalO2)=0.6, P(highO2)=0.4
  • decrease from OptimalO2 → P(lowO2)=0.6, P(OptimalO2)=0.4
  • After 4 consecutive OptimalO2 → Stabilized (terminal)

Rewards R: Shaped to guide toward OptimalO2 while penalising over-ventilation. E.g. +1 per step in OptimalO2, −1 for highO2, 0 otherwise.

Discount: \(\gamma \in (0,1)\). Episodic task — clear terminal.

(b) Design A: R=+10 for lowO2→OptimalO2. Design B: R=−10 for OptimalO2→highO2. Which do you prefer and why? [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (b)

Design A encourages recovery from hypoxia (positive reinforcement for reaching safe range). Preferred when the dominant clinical risk is under-ventilation.

Design B penalises over-ventilation directly (negative reinforcement for crossing into dangerous hyperoxia). Preferred when the dominant risk is exceeding safe thresholds.

Prefer Design B for patient safety. The stated goal explicitly includes avoiding over-ventilation. Penalising the dangerous transition (OptimalO2→highO2) aligns reward signal directly with the safety constraint, whereas Design A only rewards recovery after harm occurs.

(c) Is this episodic or continuous? Justify in ≤30 words. [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (c)

Episodic. Clear terminal state exists (4 consecutive OptimalO2 → patient stabilised). Each stabilisation attempt is one finite episode with a natural end point.

To pick the best action at state \(s\) we need \(\arg\max_a Q_\pi(s,a)\). Fix: Use an \(\varepsilon\)-soft (ε-greedy) policy that assigns positive probability \(\varepsilon/|A|\) to every action, ensuring all actions are explored and Q can be estimated for each.

(b) Two DQN stabilisation techniques and why each addresses instability. [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (b)
  1. Experience replay: Stores transitions \((s,a,r,s')\) in a replay buffer; samples random mini-batches for training. Breaks temporal correlations between consecutive samples → reduces non-stationarity → more stable gradients.
  2. Target network: A separate frozen copy of the Q-network used to compute TD targets \(r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q_{\theta^-}(s',a')\). Prevents the "chasing a moving target" feedback loop where every update shifts the target → reduces oscillation and divergence.
(c) One key difference between AlphaGo and AlphaZero regarding training data. Why does this make AlphaZero more generalisable? [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (c)

AlphaGo: Pre-trained on human expert Go game records (supervised learning phase) before self-play RL. Hard-coded human biases baked into initial policy.

AlphaZero: Starts from random play, no human data. Learns entirely through self-play.

Why more generalisable: AlphaZero is not constrained by human strategic patterns. Its architecture requires only the game rules — no domain-specific human data. The same system (without modification) achieves superhuman performance at Go, Chess, and Shogi, whereas AlphaGo is Go-specific due to its human-derived initialisation.

(d-1) Write the BC loss function for \(\pi_\theta(a|s)\). State one major failure mode with unseen states. [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (d-1)
$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \log \pi_\theta(a_i^E \mid s_i) $$

where \((s_i, a_i^E)\) are expert state-action pairs.

Failure mode: covariate shift. At test time, the learned policy makes small errors that lead to states not seen in the expert dataset. The policy has no training signal for these novel states → errors compound → catastrophic failure far from the expert distribution.

(d-2) How does DAGGER modify data collection vs BC? Why does this help with the policy's own mistakes? [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (d-2)

BC: Collects expert demos once on expert's state distribution. Policy trained offline on this fixed dataset.

DAGGER: Iteratively (1) rolls out the learned policy to collect trajectories, (2) queries the expert for the correct action at each visited state, (3) adds these to the dataset, (4) retrains.

Why it helps: DAGGER trains the policy on the states it actually visits, not just expert-visited states. When the policy makes a mistake and reaches a novel state, it now has expert-labelled training data for that state → learns to recover → distribution shift is eliminated over iterations.
