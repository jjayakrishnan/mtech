Lesson 4: Evolutionary Algorithms — GA & ACO

1. Genetic Algorithms — The Big Picture

A Genetic Algorithm (GA) mimics natural evolution. You start with a population of candidate solutions (called chromosomes or individuals), score each one using a fitness function, then breed better solutions through selection, crossover, and mutation.

Think of it like dog breeding: you pick the fastest dogs (selection), mate them (crossover), occasionally get a random surprise trait (mutation), and over many generations you end up with greyhounds.

GA Pseudocode

function GA(population, fitness): evaluate all individuals while not termination: parents = SELECT(population) children = CROSSOVER(parents) children = MUTATE(children) evaluate children population = REPLACE(population, children) return best individual found

The REPLACE step is where Generational vs Steady-State comes in — more on that below.

Key GA Components

2. Selection Methods

Selection determines which individuals become parents. The goal: fitter individuals should have a higher probability of being selected, but not exclusively — you need diversity.

Tournament Selection

Pick $k$ individuals at random from the population. The one with the highest fitness wins and becomes a parent. Repeat for the second parent.

  • $k = 2$ is most common (binary tournament)
  • Higher $k$ = more selection pressure (less diversity)
  • Simple, efficient, does not require sorting the population

Roulette Wheel (Fitness-Proportionate)

Each individual gets a slice of a roulette wheel proportional to its fitness. Spin the wheel — wherever it lands, that individual is selected.

Probability of selecting individual $i$:

$P(i) = \dfrac{f_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} f_j}$

  • Problem: if one individual has much higher fitness, it dominates (premature convergence)
  • Does not work well with negative fitness values

Rank-Based Selection

Sort individuals by fitness. Assign selection probability based on rank (not raw fitness). The worst gets rank 1, the best gets rank $N$.

  • Avoids premature convergence from super-fit outliers
  • Selection pressure is more uniform

3. Crossover Operators

Crossover (recombination) combines genetic material from two parents to produce offspring. It is the primary exploration mechanism in GA.

Single-Point Crossover

Choose one random cut point. Swap everything after the cut between the two parents.

Multi-Point Crossover

Choose $k$ cut points. Alternate segments between parents. With 2 cut points:

Uniform Crossover

For each bit position independently, flip a fair coin. Heads = take from Parent 1; Tails = take from Parent 2.

Uniform crossover is more disruptive — good for exploring widely, but can destroy good building blocks.

4. Mutation Operators

Mutation introduces random variation. It prevents the population from getting stuck at local optima. Typically applied with a low probability (e.g., $p_m = 0.01$ per bit).

Bit-Flip Mutation

For binary chromosomes: flip each bit independently with probability $p_m$.

Before: 1 0 1 1 0 ^ (bit 4 flipped) After: 1 0 1 0 0

Swap Mutation

For permutation chromosomes: pick two positions at random and swap their values.

Before: [3, 1, 4, 2, 5] Swap positions 1 and 3: After: [3, 2, 4, 1, 5]

Insert Mutation

Pick a gene at random, remove it, and insert it at a different random position.

Before: [A, B, C, D, E] Remove C, insert after D: After: [A, B, D, C, E]

5. Generational vs Steady-State GA

EXAM ERROR ALERT — You Got This Wrong Before

This distinction appeared on the exam and you confused them. Burn this into memory:

  • Generational GA: The ENTIRE population is replaced every generation. All N parents produce N children; children become the new population.
  • Steady-State GA: Only 1 or 2 individuals are replaced per iteration. The rest of the population survives unchanged.
AspectGenerational GASteady-State GA
ReplacementALL individuals replaced each generationOnly 1-2 worst replaced per iteration
Speed of changePopulation changes dramatically each stepPopulation changes gradually
DiversityHigher diversity (all new individuals)Lower diversity (most survive)
ConvergenceSlower convergenceFaster convergence
Computation per stepHigh (evaluate N individuals)Low (evaluate 1-2 individuals)
Typical useWhen exploration is criticalWhen quick convergence is desired

Memory Trick

Generational = Generation wipe — the whole generation dies and a new one takes over (like annual plants). Steady-State = Steady survival — most of the population stays alive, only the weakest die (like a wolf pack).

6. Elitism

Elitism means always copying the best individual(s) from the current generation directly into the next generation, unchanged. This guarantees the best solution found so far is never lost.

  • Typically 1-2 elite individuals are preserved
  • Used in both generational and steady-state GAs
  • Without elitism, the best solution can be destroyed by crossover/mutation
  • With elitism, GA is guaranteed to be monotonically non-decreasing in best fitness

Elitism in Practice

In a generational GA with elitism (say, elite count = 2): the 2 fittest individuals are copied unchanged into the next generation. The remaining $N-2$ slots are filled by offspring from crossover and mutation.

7. Crowding & Niching (Diversity Preservation)

EXAM ERROR ALERT — Second Confirmed Mistake

You confused crowding mechanisms on the exam. These techniques maintain population diversity by preventing all individuals from converging to the same peak.

When a fitness landscape has multiple peaks (multimodal), standard GA tends to converge to just one. Niching techniques maintain sub-populations at different peaks.

Crowding

Core idea: A new offspring replaces the most similar individual in the population (not the worst).

  • Similarity measured by genotypic distance (e.g., Hamming distance for binary strings)
  • Preserves diverse niches — new solutions only displace nearby solutions
  • The population naturally maintains individuals at multiple peaks

Fitness Sharing

Core idea: Reduce the fitness of individuals that are in crowded regions. If many individuals are clustered together, each one's effective fitness is divided by the crowd count.

$f'_i = \dfrac{f_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} sh(d_{ij})}$

where $sh(d)$ is a sharing function that equals 1 when distance is 0 and decreases to 0 beyond a niche radius $\sigma$.

  • Forces the GA to spread out across the landscape
  • Crowded areas become "expensive" — individuals move to less crowded peaks

Deterministic Crowding

Core idea: Offspring competes only with its most similar parent. The winner (higher fitness) survives.

  • Parent 1 and Parent 2 produce Child 1 and Child 2
  • Compare Parent 1 to both children — pair each parent with the most similar child
  • In each pair, the fitter one survives
  • Simple and effective — no niche radius parameter needed

8. Ant Colony Optimization (ACO)

ACO is inspired by how real ants find shortest paths. Ants wander randomly but deposit pheromone on edges they traverse. Over time, shorter paths accumulate more pheromone (ants complete them faster, depositing more frequently), creating a positive feedback loop.

How ACO Works

  1. Initialize pheromone on all edges to a small constant
  2. Each ant builds a solution by choosing edges probabilistically
  3. After all ants complete their tours, update pheromones (evaporate + deposit)
  4. Repeat until convergence or iteration limit

Edge Selection Probability

The probability that ant $k$ at node $i$ chooses edge $(i,j)$:

$p_{ij}^k = \dfrac{\tau_{ij}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{ij}^\beta}{\displaystyle\sum_{l \in \text{allowed}} \tau_{il}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{il}^\beta}$

  • $\tau_{ij}$ = pheromone on edge $(i,j)$ — learned desirability
  • $\eta_{ij}$ = heuristic value, typically $1/d_{ij}$ (inverse distance) — greedy desirability
  • $\alpha$ = weight of pheromone influence
  • $\beta$ = weight of heuristic influence

Pheromone Update Rule

$\tau_{ij} \leftarrow (1 - \rho) \cdot \tau_{ij} + \Delta\tau_{ij}$

  • $\rho$ = evaporation rate ($0 < \rho < 1$). Old pheromone fades over time.
  • $\Delta\tau_{ij}$ = pheromone deposited by ants that used edge $(i,j)$
  • Typically $\Delta\tau_{ij} = \sum_k Q / L_k$ where $L_k$ is the tour length of ant $k$

Intuition

Evaporation prevents convergence to suboptimal paths — bad paths lose pheromone. Deposit reinforces good paths — shorter tours deposit more pheromone per edge. The combination creates emergent intelligence: no single ant knows the optimal path, but the colony converges to it.

Worked Example — One GA Generation

Setup

Population of 4 binary chromosomes (length 5):

IndividualChromosomeFitness
A1 0 1 1 03
B0 1 1 0 12
C1 1 0 0 14
D0 0 1 1 11

Step 1: Tournament Selection (k=2)

Pick 2 random individuals, the fitter one wins.

  • Tournament 1: Draw C (fitness 4) and B (fitness 2) → C wins → Parent 1 = "11001"
  • Tournament 2: Draw A (fitness 3) and D (fitness 1) → A wins → Parent 2 = "10110"

Step 2: Single-Point Crossover (cut at position 3)

Parent 1 (C): 1 1 0 | 0 1 Parent 2 (A): 1 0 1 | 1 0 ─────────── Child 1: 1 1 0 | 1 0 = "11010" Child 2: 1 0 1 | 0 1 = "10101"

Step 3: Mutation (bit-flip, p=0.2 per bit)

Suppose bit 2 of Child 1 is flipped:

Child 1 before: 1 1 0 1 0 ^ (bit 2 flipped) Child 1 after: 1 0 0 1 0 = "10010"

Step 4: Evaluate

Suppose fitness("10010") = 2, fitness("10101") = 3.

Step 5: Replacement (Generational with Elitism)

Elite: keep C (fitness 4). Fill remaining 3 slots with children + next best from offspring/parents.

New population: C="11001"(4), Child2="10101"(3), A="10110"(3), Child1="10010"(2).

Key Insight

Notice how the best individual C survived via elitism, even though it was a parent. Without elitism, it could have been lost entirely.

Closed-Book: Memorise This

ACO Formulas

  • Pheromone update: $\tau_{ij} \leftarrow (1-\rho)\cdot\tau_{ij} + \Delta\tau_{ij}$
  • Edge probability: $p_{ij} = \dfrac{\tau_{ij}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{ij}^\beta}{\sum_l \tau_{il}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{il}^\beta}$
  • $\tau$ = pheromone, $\eta$ = heuristic (1/distance), $\rho$ = evaporation rate

Generational vs Steady-State (THE DISTINCTION)

  • Generational = Replace ALL. Entire population replaced every generation.
  • Steady-State = Replace FEW. Only 1-2 individuals replaced per iteration.

Crowding & Niching (THE DISTINCTION)

  • Crowding: Offspring replaces the most SIMILAR individual in population.
  • Fitness Sharing: Penalize (reduce) fitness of individuals in crowded regions.
  • Deterministic Crowding: Offspring competes only with its most similar PARENT.

Selection Methods

  • Tournament: Pick k random, best wins
  • Roulette: Probability proportional to fitness
  • Rank: Probability proportional to rank (not raw fitness)

Crossover Types

  • Single-point: One cut, swap tails
  • Multi-point: k cuts, alternate segments
  • Uniform: Each bit chosen independently from either parent

Elitism

  • Always preserve best individual(s) across generations
  • Guarantees best fitness never decreases
