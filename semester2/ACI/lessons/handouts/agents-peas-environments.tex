\section{What this session covers}
% =============================================================================

Session 1 of ACI sets the stage for the whole course.
It answers four big questions.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item What exactly is \emph{artificial intelligence}? Four perspectives.
  \item What is an \emph{agent} and what does it do?
  \item How do we describe an agent's task? The \emph{PEAS} framework.
  \item What kinds of \emph{environments} can an agent live in? Six properties.
\end{enumerate}

The textbook is \emph{Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach}, 4th edition,
by Stuart Russell and Peter Norvig (referred to as T1 in the course).
Section 1.1 covers perspectives on AI; Chapter 2 covers agents and environments.

\section{Four perspectives on artificial intelligence}
% =============================================================================

When researchers try to define AI, they split on two questions.
First: should a machine \emph{think} like a human, or should it reason
\emph{correctly}? Second: should it \emph{behave} like a human, or should it
act \emph{rationally}? Crossing those two choices gives four cells.

\begin{intuition}
Think of it as a two-by-two table. One axis is \emph{human} vs.\ \emph{rational}.
The other is \emph{thought} vs.\ \emph{behaviour}.
Most of this course lives in cell four: acting rationally.
\end{intuition}

\medskip
\begin{adjustbox}{max width=\linewidth}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Human performance} & \textbf{Rational performance} \\
\midrule
\textbf{Thought / Reasoning} & (1) Thinking humanly & (3) Thinking rationally \\
\textbf{Acting / Behaviour}  & (2) Acting humanly  & (4) Acting rationally \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{adjustbox}
\medskip

\textbf{(1) Thinking humanly --- the cognitive modelling approach.}
The goal is to understand and replicate how humans think.
We study human cognition through introspection, psychological experiments,
and brain imaging.
Deep Blue beat Garry Kasparov at chess, but it does not think like a human.
This approach matters for intelligent tutoring systems and human--computer
interaction, where human-like reasoning is important.

\textbf{(2) Acting humanly --- the Turing Test approach.}
Alan Turing proposed a test in 1950.
An interrogator exchanges text messages with two hidden parties: a human
and a computer.
If the interrogator cannot tell which is which, the computer passes.
To pass, the machine needs natural language processing (NLP), knowledge
representation, automated reasoning, and machine learning.

\textbf{(3) Thinking rationally --- the laws-of-thought approach.}
Aristotle formalised logic in the third century BC.
He introduced \vocab{syllogisms} --- argument structures that guarantee a
correct conclusion given true premises.
The classic example: ``All humans are mortal; Socrates is human; so
Socrates is mortal.''
The field of formal logic tried to codify all rational thinking.
Its problem: not every intelligent action is the result of logical
deliberation, and even a few hundred facts can exhaust a computer.

\textbf{(4) Acting rationally --- the rational agent approach.}
This is the approach this course takes.
A \vocab{rational agent} acts to achieve the best expected outcome.
It does not have to think exactly like a human.
It just has to make good decisions.

\begin{everyday}
Aeronautical engineers do not build planes that flap their wings like
birds. They find the best way to fly. AI researchers do the same: they
build the best decision-maker, not a human imitation.
\end{everyday}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
All modern ML systems are designed as rational agents.
They learn a policy that maximises a reward or loss objective.
None of them is required to think the way a human does.

\begin{keytake}
The four perspectives: (1) thinking humanly, (2) acting humanly, (3) thinking
rationally, (4) acting rationally. This course focuses on (4).
\end{keytake}

\section{Agents and environments}
% =============================================================================

Everything in this course is built around one idea.

\begin{intuition}
An \vocab{agent} is anything that perceives its environment through
\vocab{sensors} and acts on that environment through \vocab{actuators}.
The agent reads its sensors, decides what to do, and sends a command to
its actuators. That is the complete loop.
\end{intuition}

\begin{everyday}
You are an agent. Your sensors are your eyes, ears, and other senses.
Your actuators are your hands, legs, and voice.
You perceive the world and act on it.
A robot vacuum cleaner is also an agent.
Its sensors are a dirt detector and a location sensor.
Its actuators are its wheels and its suction motor.
\end{everyday}

Three kinds of agent appear in the slides.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Human agent.} Sensors: eyes, ears, skin, nose.
        Actuators: hands, legs, mouth.
  \item \textbf{Robotic agent.} Sensors: cameras, infrared range finders.
        Actuators: various motors.
  \item \textbf{Software (AI) agent.} Sensors: text input, data streams.
        Actuators: text output, API calls, control signals.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The agent function and agent program}

At each moment the agent has seen a sequence of percepts.
We call that the \vocab{percept history}.
The \vocab{agent function} maps any percept history to an action:

\[
  f \colon \mathcal{P}^{*} \to \mathcal{A}
\]

Here $\mathcal{P}^{*}$ (read: ``the set of all percept histories'') is every
possible sequence of percepts the agent could have seen.
$\mathcal{A}$ is the set of possible actions.
So $f$ is the complete specification of what the agent would do in any situation.

The \vocab{agent program} is the actual code that runs on a physical machine
and produces $f$.
The short equation the slides give is:

\[
  \text{agent} = \text{architecture} + \text{program}
\]

The architecture is the hardware (CPU, sensors, actuators).
The program is the software that makes decisions.

\housefig{figures/fig_agent_loop.png}{The agent--environment loop. The agent
reads percepts from the environment via sensors and sends actions back via
actuators. One cycle repeats every time step.}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
In reinforcement learning (RL), the agent function $f$ is called the
\emph{policy}, usually written $\pi$.
The policy maps an observation $o_t$ (or history) to an action $a_t$.
Training an RL agent means finding the policy $\pi$ that maximises
expected cumulative reward.

\begin{keytake}
Agent = architecture + program.
The agent function $f\colon \mathcal{P}^{*} \to \mathcal{A}$ maps the full
percept history to an action.
\end{keytake}

\section{Rational agents}
% =============================================================================

Knowing what an agent \emph{is} does not yet say what a \emph{good} agent does.

\begin{intuition}
A rational agent does the \emph{right thing}.
The right thing is the action expected to score best on the
\vocab{performance measure} --- the scoring rule that tells us what counts
as success.
\end{intuition}

\begin{everyday}
Think of a student taking an exam. The performance measure is the final
mark. A rational student picks answers that maximise the expected mark
given the time and knowledge available. They do not need to know every
answer; they just need to make the best use of what they know.
\end{everyday}

\subsection{Performance measure}

A \vocab{performance measure} is an objective criterion for success.
It is defined from the \emph{outside} --- by the designer who wants certain
outcomes --- not from the agent's internal preferences.

\textbf{Example: vacuum cleaner.}
The performance measure could be any of these:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Amount of dirt cleaned up.
  \item Amount of electricity used (we want this small).
  \item Amount of noise generated (we want this small).
  \item Time taken to finish.
\end{itemize}
A designer must choose and combine these carefully.
An agent that scores well on one might score poorly on another.

\housefig{figures/fig_performance_measure.png}{Four components of a
vacuum-cleaner performance measure. High bars are good for dirt cleaned;
low bars are good for energy, noise, and time. The designer must decide
how to weight them --- the agent then tries to maximise the combined score.}

\subsection{The ideal rational agent}

The slides give this definition.

\medskip
\begin{tcolorbox}[enhanced,colback=intuFill,colframe=intuFrame,boxrule=0.6pt,arc=2.4pt]
``For each possible percept sequence, a rational agent does whatever action
is expected to maximise its performance measure.
It acts on evidence perceived \emph{so far} and on built-in knowledge.''
\end{tcolorbox}
\medskip

Three words in this definition deserve attention.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Expected.} The agent may face uncertainty.
        It picks the action with the best \emph{expected} score, not a
        guaranteed one.
  \item \textbf{So far.} The agent acts on what it has seen up to now.
        It is not omniscient --- it cannot see the future.
  \item \textbf{Built-in knowledge.} The agent may have prior knowledge
        built in by its designer (e.g.\ a map, rules of a game).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rationality is not omniscience}

\vocab{Omniscience} means knowing everything, including the future outcome of
every action.
Rationality does not require omniscience.
A rational agent makes the best decision \emph{given its current information}.
If the outcome turns out bad, that does not make the agent irrational ---
it may simply have been unlucky.

An agent is \vocab{autonomous} if its behaviour depends on its own experience
rather than only on the prior knowledge built in by its designer.
An autonomous agent can learn and adapt.

\subsection{Bounded rationality}

Perfect rationality requires searching over all possible actions and all
possible futures.
That is computationally impossible in most real settings.
\vocab{Bounded rationality} is the practical version: do the best you
can given limited time and limited computational resources.
The agent optimises its performance measure \emph{subject to} a computational
budget.

\begin{worked}{Vacuum cleaner rationality}
The vacuum cleaner is in room A, which is dirty. Room B's state is unknown.
\begin{steps}
  \item \textbf{State the setup.}
        Percept: $[\text{A, Dirty}]$.
        Actions available: \textit{Left}, \textit{Right}, \textit{Suck},
        \textit{NoOp}.
  \item \textbf{Identify the rational action.}
        Room A is dirty. Sucking cleans it right now.
        Moving to B first would leave A dirty longer.
        So the rational action is \textbf{Suck}.
  \item \textbf{Check the next step.}
        After Suck, the new percept might be $[\text{A, Clean}]$.
        Now the agent does not know whether B is dirty.
        A rational agent explores --- it moves Right to check.
  \item \textbf{Sense-check.}
        This matches the slide's percept--action table:
        $[\text{A, Dirty}] \to \text{Suck}$ and
        $[\text{A, Clean}] \to \text{Right}$.
\end{steps}
The key insight: rationality means acting on \emph{available} evidence, not
on perfect knowledge of the world.
\end{worked}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
Every supervised and reinforcement learning algorithm is a search for a
rational policy.
In RL, the Bellman equation formalises expected future reward.
That is exactly ``expected performance on the basis of evidence so far''
from the definition above.

\begin{keytake}
A rational agent maximises \emph{expected} performance on the basis of
evidence so far. Rationality $\ne$ omniscience. Bounded rationality is the
real-world version: best action given limited computation.
\end{keytake}

\section{PEAS: specifying the task environment}
% =============================================================================

Before we can design an agent we need to describe its task precisely.
The standard tool is \textbf{PEAS}:
\textbf{P}erformance measure,
\textbf{E}nvironment,
\textbf{A}ctuators,
\textbf{S}ensors.

\begin{intuition}
PEAS is a job description for the agent.
\emph{Performance} says what success looks like.
\emph{Environment} says where the agent lives.
\emph{Actuators} say what it can do.
\emph{Sensors} say what it can see.
Write all four before writing a single line of agent code.
\end{intuition}

\begin{everyday}
Imagine hiring a taxi driver. Before the interview you write a job spec.
Success means safe, fast, legal, comfortable trips --- that is P.
The workplace is city roads with other traffic and pedestrians --- that is E.
The tools are a steering wheel, accelerator, brake, and horn --- that is A.
The information comes from eyes, GPS, and a speedometer --- that is S.
PEAS is that job spec, but for an AI system.
\end{everyday}

\begin{worked}{PEAS for the automated taxi driver (slide example)}
The slide lists this agent explicitly. We work through all four letters.
\begin{steps}
  \item \textbf{Performance measure.}
        Safe trip; arrive at the correct destination; obey traffic laws;
        travel quickly; ride is comfortable; maximise profit.
  \item \textbf{Environment.}
        Roads, other vehicles, pedestrians, weather, traffic signals,
        customers, maps, GPS data.
  \item \textbf{Actuators.}
        Steering wheel, accelerator pedal, brake pedal, horn, turn signals.
  \item \textbf{Sensors.}
        Cameras (front, rear, side), sonar or LIDAR for distance,
        GPS receiver, speedometer, odometer, engine sensors, keyboard
        (for passenger input).
\end{steps}
Full PEAS in one line: \textbf{P} safe/fast/legal/comfortable trip;
\textbf{E} roads, traffic, pedestrians, weather;
\textbf{A} steering, accelerator, brake, horn, signals;
\textbf{S} cameras, LIDAR, GPS, speedometer, odometer.
\end{worked}

\begin{worked}{PEAS for the vacuum cleaner (slide example)}
The vacuum lives in a two-room world with rooms A and B.
\begin{steps}
  \item \textbf{Performance measure.}
        Maximise cleanliness (number of clean squares over time).
        Minimise energy consumption.
  \item \textbf{Environment.}
        Two rooms, A and B. Each room is either Clean or Dirty.
        The agent starts in one of them.
  \item \textbf{Actuators.}
        \textit{Left} (move to A), \textit{Right} (move to B),
        \textit{Suck} (clean current room), \textit{NoOp} (do nothing).
  \item \textbf{Sensors.}
        Location sensor: which room am I in?
        Dirt sensor: is the current room Dirty or Clean?
\end{steps}
Full PEAS: \textbf{P} clean squares, energy;
\textbf{E} 2-room grid, each dirty or clean;
\textbf{A} Left, Right, Suck, NoOp;
\textbf{S} location, dirt detector.
\end{worked}

\housefig{figures/fig_peas_taxi.png}{PEAS for the automated taxi. Each letter
of PEAS is a separate design decision. Get the performance measure wrong and
the agent optimises the wrong thing.}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
Every RL framework starts with a PEAS-like specification.
In OpenAI Gym / Gymnasium:
\emph{performance measure} is the reward function,
\emph{environment} is the \texttt{env} object,
\emph{actuators} are the action space,
\emph{sensors} are the observation space.

\begin{watchout}
The performance measure must be defined from \emph{outside} the agent ---
by the designer, not by the agent itself.
If you let the agent define its own performance measure it will find
clever ways to score well without doing what you actually wanted.
This is the \emph{reward hacking} problem in RL.
\end{watchout}

\begin{keytake}
PEAS = Performance, Environment, Actuators, Sensors.
Write all four before building any agent.
The performance measure is set by the designer; the agent tries to maximise it.
\end{keytake}

\section{Properties of task environments}
% =============================================================================

Different environments are harder or easier for an agent to handle.
We describe every environment along six independent dimensions.

\begin{intuition}
These six pairs are a checklist. Read the problem statement, tick each
pair, and you have a complete picture of how hard the design problem is.
Harder environments demand more sophisticated agents.
\end{intuition}

\begin{adjustbox}{max width=\linewidth}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Dimension} & \textbf{One end} & \textbf{Other end} \\
\midrule
Observability & Fully observable & Partially observable \\
Number of agents & Single-agent & Multi-agent (competitive or cooperative) \\
Outcome of actions & Deterministic & Stochastic \\
Episode structure & Episodic & Sequential \\
Change over time & Static & Dynamic \\
State / action space & Discrete & Continuous \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{adjustbox}

\medskip

\textbf{Fully observable} means the agent's sensors give it the complete
state at every step.
Chess: you can see every piece --- fully observable.
\textbf{Partially observable} means some state is hidden.
Poker: you cannot see your opponents' cards --- partially observable.

\textbf{Deterministic} means the next state is fully determined by the
current state and the chosen action.
No randomness at all.
\textbf{Stochastic} means there is uncertainty: the outcome may vary even
if you take the same action in the same state.
Rolling dice, slippery floors, sensor noise.

\textbf{Episodic} means each task episode is independent.
What happened in episode 1 has no effect on episode 2.
Sorting an email inbox: each email is a fresh decision.
\textbf{Sequential} means past actions affect future states.
Chess: every move changes what moves are legal later.

\textbf{Static} means the environment does not change while the agent is
thinking.
A crossword puzzle waits for you.
\textbf{Dynamic} means it can change at any time --- even mid-deliberation.
Driving: other cars keep moving while you think.
A \textbf{semi-dynamic} environment does not change, but the agent's
performance score changes with time (e.g.\ a timed exam).

\textbf{Discrete} means a finite (or countably infinite) set of states and
actions.
Chess: fixed number of board positions and legal moves.
\textbf{Continuous} means states or actions take real-valued (numerical)
values.
Self-driving car: steering angle, speed, and position are all continuous.

\begin{worked}{Classify the vacuum-cleaner world}
The two-room vacuum world from the slides. We justify each property.
\begin{steps}
  \item \textbf{Observability.} The agent sees its location and the
        dirt status of its current room. But it cannot see room B while
        standing in A. \textbf{Partially observable.}
  \item \textbf{Number of agents.} Only one vacuum.
        \textbf{Single-agent.}
  \item \textbf{Determinism.} In the basic version, sucking always cleans.
        Moving always changes location.
        \textbf{Deterministic.}
  \item \textbf{Episode structure.} Does what happened last time matter?
        If rooms can get dirty again after cleaning, past actions matter.
        \textbf{Sequential.}
  \item \textbf{Change over time.} In the basic version the rooms do not
        get dirtier while the agent thinks (no time pressure changes state).
        \textbf{Static.}
  \item \textbf{State space.} Two rooms, two dirt states each = 4 world
        states; 4 actions.
        \textbf{Discrete.}
\end{steps}
Result: Partially observable, Single-agent, Deterministic,
Sequential, Static, Discrete.
\end{worked}

\begin{worked}{Classify online chess against a human}
The slides also ask about chess. We justify each property.
\begin{steps}
  \item \textbf{Observability.} Both players see the full board.
        \textbf{Fully observable.}
  \item \textbf{Number of agents.} Two players, each trying to win.
        \textbf{Multi-agent, competitive.}
  \item \textbf{Determinism.} Pieces move to exactly the stated square.
        No dice. \textbf{Deterministic.}
  \item \textbf{Episode structure.} Every past move restricts future
        legal moves. \textbf{Sequential.}
  \item \textbf{Change over time.} The board does not change while you
        are thinking. \textbf{Static.}
  \item \textbf{State space.} Finite number of squares and piece types.
        \textbf{Discrete.}
\end{steps}
Result: Fully observable, Multi-agent competitive, Deterministic,
Sequential, Static, Discrete.
\end{worked}

\housefig{figures/fig_ai_perspectives.png}{Four perspectives on AI and the
six environment dimensions. The cell labelled ``acting rationally'' is where
this course lives; harder environments (bottom-right) need more capable agents.}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
RL researchers use these six dimensions to describe benchmark environments.
Atari games: fully observable, single-agent, deterministic, episodic,
dynamic, discrete --- a good starting point.
Real-world robotics: partially observable, single-agent, stochastic,
sequential, dynamic, continuous --- much harder.
Knowing the properties tells you which algorithms will work.

\begin{watchout}
Two common mix-ups at the exam.

First: \emph{static} vs.\ \emph{deterministic}.
Static means the environment does not change while the agent is thinking.
Deterministic means there is no randomness in the outcome of actions.
An environment can be static \emph{and} stochastic.
A card-shuffling puzzle is static (nothing moves while you think) but
stochastic (you draw a random card).

Second: \emph{multi-agent} does not always mean \emph{competitive}.
Two robots cooperating to carry a sofa are multi-agent but cooperative.
Two chess players are multi-agent and competitive.
\end{watchout}

\begin{keytake}
Six dimensions: (1) fully vs.\ partially observable; (2) single vs.\
multi-agent; (3) deterministic vs.\ stochastic.
Also: (4) episodic vs.\ sequential; (5) static vs.\ dynamic;
(6) discrete vs.\ continuous.
Harder environments demand more sophisticated agents.
\end{keytake}

\section{The agent--environment percept table}
% =============================================================================

The slide shows the vacuum cleaner's full percept--action mapping.
This table is the agent function $f$ written out explicitly.

\begin{intuition}
Every row is one possible percept (or percept history). The right column
gives the action the agent takes. An agent that always acts rationally
fills in this table optimally. For simple environments we can write the
whole table down. For complex ones we need a program to approximate it.
\end{intuition}

\begin{adjustbox}{max width=\linewidth}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Percept sequence (what the agent has seen so far)} & \textbf{Action} \\
\midrule
$[\text{A, Clean}]$                              & Right \\
$[\text{A, Dirty}]$                              & Suck  \\
$[\text{B, Clean}]$                              & Left  \\
$[\text{B, Dirty}]$                              & Suck  \\
$[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Clean}]$            & Right \\
$[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Dirty}]$            & Suck  \\
$\vdots$                                         & $\vdots$ \\
$[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Clean}]$ & Right \\
$[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Clean}],[\text{A, Dirty}]$ & Suck  \\
$\vdots$                                         & $\vdots$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{adjustbox}

\medskip

Notice two things. First, the table grows without bound: there is one row
for every possible percept history, and histories can be arbitrarily long.
Second, the last action is always based on the \emph{whole history}, not
just the latest percept.
A simple reflex agent ignores history; a model-based agent summarises it.

\housefig{figures/fig_vacuum_table.png}{The vacuum-cleaner percept--action
table. Each row is one percept history. The agent always picks the action
in the right column. The table is the agent function written out in full.}

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
A look-up table agent is the simplest possible policy.
In RL, the Q-table in tabular Q-learning is exactly this: a table mapping
(state, action) pairs to expected rewards.
For large state spaces, a neural network compresses the table into a
parameterised function.

\begin{keytake}
The percept--action table \emph{is} the agent function.
It maps every possible percept history to an action.
Real agents approximate this table because it is too large to store.
\end{keytake}

\section{A brief history and foundations of AI}
% =============================================================================

Understanding where AI came from helps you understand why the rational
agent view won.

\textbf{Key milestones.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item 1950: Alan Turing proposes the Turing Test.
  \item 1956: John McCarthy coins the term ``Artificial Intelligence''
        at the Dartmouth Conference.
  \item 1957--1973: Early optimism. First programs, first robots.
        First ``AI Winter'' follows (funding cuts, overpromising).
  \item 1980--1987: Expert systems boom.
        Second ``AI Winter'' follows.
  \item 1997: IBM Deep Blue defeats world chess champion Kasparov.
  \item 2006: Deep Belief Networks (Hinton et al.).
  \item 2012: AlexNet wins ImageNet; deep learning era begins.
  \item 2017: Transformer architecture (``Attention Is All You Need'').
  \item 2022: ChatGPT launches; large language models go mainstream.
  \item 2025: DeepSeek R1 and rapid advances in agentic AI.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What changed in the last decade?}
Three forces converged: \emph{data} (internet scale), \emph{compute}
(GPUs, TPUs), and \emph{algorithms} (deep learning, transformers).
Each alone was not enough; all three together unlocked modern AI.

\housefig{figures/fig_ai_timeline.png}{Key milestones in AI history. Two
``winters'' (funding cuts) followed periods of overpromising. Modern AI
took off when data, compute, and deep-learning algorithms converged.}

\textbf{Foundations of AI.}
AI draws on many fields.
These include philosophy (logic, rationality), mathematics (probability,
computation), and economics (decision theory, game theory).
Neuroscience, psychology, computer engineering, and linguistics all
contribute too.

\textbf{Where this shows up in ML.}
Transformer-based large language models are the direct result of that
2017 milestone. Understanding the history helps you see why the field
is structured the way it is --- and where the next big shift might come.

\begin{keytake}
AI has gone through cycles of optimism and winters.
Modern AI is powered by the convergence of data, compute, and algorithms.
The rational agent framework is the unifying idea across all of it.
\end{keytake}

\section{AI Agents vs.\ Agentic AI}
% =============================================================================

The slides draw a distinction that students often miss.

\begin{intuition}
An \vocab{AI agent} solves a specific task using learned or programmed
rules. \vocab{Agentic AI} is a network of agents with high autonomy:
they plan, delegate sub-tasks, adapt, and coordinate without a human
directing every step.
The key difference is the \emph{level of autonomy}.
\end{intuition}

\textbf{Example: customer support.}
An AI agent answers frequently-asked questions using a knowledge base.
It has fixed inputs and outputs.
An agentic AI system would also escalate complaints, analyse customer
sentiment, adapt its scripts, and coordinate with backend systems to
resolve problems --- all autonomously.
The orchestration and planning are themselves learned, not hard-coded.

This course focuses on rational agents: we study the principles
(search, logic, probability) that underlie all AI systems.
We do not go into the specifics of modern agentic pipelines
(MCP, tool-calling, LLM orchestration).

\begin{keytake}
An AI agent solves a fixed task by perceiving and acting.
Agentic AI is a higher-autonomy system where planning and coordination
are also automated. This course focuses on the rational-agent foundation.
\end{keytake}

\section*{Glossary \& symbols}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Glossary \& symbols}
% =============================================================================

\noindent\textbf{Key terms.}
\begin{description}[leftmargin=8.5em,style=nextline,font=\normalfont\bfseries\color{navy}]
  \item[agent] anything that perceives its environment and acts on it.
  \item[percept] a single reading from the agent's sensors at one time step.
  \item[actuator] the part of the agent that produces actions (hands, wheels, API calls).
  \item[agent function] the mapping $f\colon\mathcal{P}^{*}\to\mathcal{A}$ from percept history to action.
  \item[agent program] the code that implements the agent function on real hardware.
  \item[performance measure] the scoring rule that says what counts as success.
  \item[rational agent] an agent that picks the action expected to maximise its performance measure.
  \item[omniscience] knowing the actual outcome of every action --- not required for rationality.
  \item[autonomy] the degree to which an agent's behaviour is driven by its own experience, not built-in knowledge.
  \item[bounded rationality] doing the best you can given limited computation and time.
  \item[PEAS] Performance, Environment, Actuators, Sensors --- the four-part task description.
  \item[fully observable] the sensors reveal the complete state of the environment.
  \item[partially observable] some state is hidden from the agent's sensors.
  \item[deterministic] the next state is fully determined by the current state and action.
  \item[stochastic] the outcome of an action has some randomness.
  \item[episodic] each decision episode is independent; past episodes do not affect future ones.
  \item[sequential] past actions affect future states.
  \item[static] the environment does not change while the agent deliberates.
  \item[dynamic] the environment can change at any time, even mid-deliberation.
  \item[discrete] states and actions come from a finite (or countable) set.
  \item[continuous] states or actions take real-valued numbers.
  \item[Turing Test] a test of machine intelligence proposed by Alan Turing (1950).
        A machine passes if an interrogator cannot tell it apart from a human in text conversation.
  \item[syllogism] a logical argument form with two premises and a conclusion; introduced by Aristotle.
\end{description}

\end{tabular}
\end{center}
