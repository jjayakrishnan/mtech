1. Policies: The Agent's Decision Rule

A policy is a mapping from states to actions. It tells the agent what to do in every situation. Formally:

Definition

\(\pi(a \mid s)\) = probability of choosing action \(a\) when in state \(s\).

Plain English

Think of a policy as a recipe book. Given the current situation (state), the policy tells you what action to take — either one specific action (deterministic), or a probability distribution over several actions (stochastic).

Deterministic vs Stochastic

Deterministic policy: \(\pi(s) = a\) — one fixed action per state. Example: "always go right in state 3."

Stochastic policy: \(\pi(a \mid s)\) gives probabilities. Example: "in state 3, go right with probability 0.7, left with probability 0.3." This is more general — a deterministic policy is a stochastic policy where one action has probability 1.

Exam tip

A "random policy" (equiprobable) means \(\pi(a|s) = 1/|\mathcal{A}(s)|\) for all actions. In a 4-action gridworld, each direction has probability 0.25.

2. State-Value Function: vπ(s)

Once you have a policy, you can ask: "How good is it to be in state \(s\) if I follow policy \(\pi\) from now on?"

Definition

\(v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \mid S_t = s]\)

Plain English

The state-value function is like asking: "If I'm standing at location \(s\) and I follow my recipe book (\(\pi\)) forever, what's my average total score going to be?" The expectation averages over all the randomness — both the policy's random choices and the environment's random transitions.

Here \(G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma R_{t+2} + \gamma^2 R_{t+3} + \ldots\) is the discounted return from time step \(t\).

3. Action-Value Function: qπ(s, a)

Sometimes you want more detail: "How good is it to take action \(a\) in state \(s\), and then follow policy \(\pi\)?"

Definition

\(q_\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \mid S_t = s, A_t = a]\)

Plain English

The action-value function is like asking: "If I'm at location \(s\), I take one specific action \(a\) (maybe not what my recipe book says), but then I follow the recipe book after that — what's my average total score?"

Relationship between v and q

The state-value is just the weighted average of action-values:

\[v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) \, q_\pi(s, a)\]

You average \(q\) over all actions, weighted by how often the policy picks each one.

4. Bellman Equation for vπ

The key insight: the value of a state relates recursively to the values of its successor states.

Plain English first

Your total score from state \(s\) = (weighted average over actions your policy might take) of [(weighted average over places you might land) of (immediate reward + discounted future value of where you land)].

Bellman Expectation Equation for vπ

\[v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s', r} p(s', r \mid s, a)\bigl[r + \gamma \, v_\pi(s')\bigr]\]
Sum over actions (weighted by policy) → sum over next states/rewards (weighted by dynamics) → immediate reward + discounted future.

This is a system of linear equations — one equation per state. If you have \(|\mathcal{S}|\) states, you have \(|\mathcal{S}|\) equations in \(|\mathcal{S}|\) unknowns.

5. Bellman Equation for qπ

Similarly, the action-value has its own recursive form:

Plain English first

Your total score after taking action \(a\) in state \(s\) = (weighted average over places you land) of [immediate reward + discounted (weighted average over actions the policy would take in the next state) of (action-value at next state)].

Bellman Expectation Equation for qπ

\[q_\pi(s, a) = \sum_{s', r} p(s', r \mid s, a)\Bigl[r + \gamma \sum_{a'} \pi(a' \mid s') \, q_\pi(s', a')\Bigr]\]
Sum over next states → reward + discounted [sum over next actions weighted by policy → q at next state-action].
Spot the structure

The v equation has two nested sums: outer over actions (\(\pi\)), inner over transitions (\(p\)). The q equation starts inside the action already, so it has one outer sum over transitions, with a nested sum over next-state actions.

Key intuition

v backup: starts at a state, fans out to actions (policy), then to next-states (dynamics). q backup: starts at a state-action, fans out to next-states (dynamics), then to next-actions (policy). Each diagram mirrors the structure of its Bellman equation.

7. Gridworld Example (Sutton & Barto 3.5)

A 5×5 grid with two special states. Under an equiprobable random policy (\(\pi(a|s) = 0.25\) for all 4 directions), with \(\gamma = 0.9\):

Special states

State A (row 0, col 1): Any action sends you to A' (row 4, col 1) with reward +10.
State B (row 0, col 3): Any action sends you to B' (row 2, col 3) with reward +5.
Normal moves give reward 0. Moving off-grid keeps you in place with reward -1.

Try it yourself: Verify vπ(row 2, col 2) ≈ 0.7

Task: Using the Bellman equation for \(v_\pi\), verify that the value of state (2,2) is approximately 0.7.

State (2,2) is in the middle of the grid. Under random policy, each of the 4 actions has probability 0.25. All transitions are deterministic (move one cell in chosen direction). Reward is 0 for all moves from this state (none go off-grid).

Neighbors: Up → (1,2): v=2.3 | Down → (3,2): v=-0.4 | Left → (2,1): v=0.7 | Right → (2,3): v=0.7

Solution:

\[v_\pi(2,2) = 0.25 \times [0 + 0.9 \times 2.3] + 0.25 \times [0 + 0.9 \times (-0.4)]\] \[+ 0.25 \times [0 + 0.9 \times 0.7] + 0.25 \times [0 + 0.9 \times 0.7]\]

\[= 0.25 \times [2.07 + (-0.36) + 0.63 + 0.63]\]

\[= 0.25 \times 2.97 = 0.7425 \approx 0.7 \; \checkmark\]

Try it yourself: Why is v(A) = 8.8, not 10?

Task: State A gives +10 reward and teleports to A' (row 4, col 1). Why isn't v(A) = 10?

Solution: Because the value includes the discounted future from where you land:

\[v_\pi(A) = 10 + \gamma \cdot v_\pi(A') = 10 + 0.9 \times (-1.3) = 10 - 1.17 = 8.83 \approx 8.8\]

A' is at position (4,1) which has value -1.3 under the random policy (bottom row, near edges). The +10 immediate reward gets dragged down slightly by the poor future prospects of the landing spot.

8. Optimal Value Functions

Among all possible policies, there exists at least one that is as good or better than all others in every state. Its value functions are the optimal value functions.

Definitions

Optimal state-value: \(v_*(s) = \max_\pi \, v_\pi(s)\) for all \(s \in \mathcal{S}\)

Optimal action-value: \(q_*(s, a) = \max_\pi \, q_\pi(s, a)\) for all \(s, a\)

Plain English

\(v_*(s)\) answers: "What's the best possible expected return from state \(s\), if I could use the very best policy?" And \(q_*(s,a)\) answers: "What's the best expected return if I take action \(a\) in state \(s\) and then behave optimally afterward?"

Once you know \(q_*\), finding the optimal policy is trivial: in each state, just pick the action with the highest \(q_*\) value.

\[\pi_*(a \mid s) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } a = \arg\max_{a'} q_*(s, a') \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

9-10. Bellman Optimality Equations

The optimal value functions satisfy their own recursive equations — but with max instead of the policy-weighted sum.

Bellman Optimality Equation for v*

\[v_*(s) = \max_a \sum_{s', r} p(s', r \mid s, a)\bigl[r + \gamma \, v_*(s')\bigr]\]
No policy sum — instead, pick the single best action (max). Then average over transitions.

Bellman Optimality Equation for q*

\[q_*(s, a) = \sum_{s', r} p(s', r \mid s, a)\Bigl[r + \gamma \max_{a'} q_*(s', a')\Bigr]\]
Average over transitions, then at the next state pick the best next action (max).
Plain English

The optimality equations say: "The best possible value of a state equals the value you get by picking the best available action." No averaging over a policy's randomness — you always take the best action.

Try it yourself: Bellman optimality for a simple 2-state MDP

Setup: Two states {A, B}. In state A, two actions: "stay" (go to A, reward 2) and "go" (go to B, reward 0). In state B, one action: "back" (go to A, reward 1). \(\gamma = 0.5\). Find \(v_*(A)\) and \(v_*(B)\).

Solution:

Bellman optimality equations:

\[v_*(A) = \max\{2 + 0.5 \cdot v_*(A), \; 0 + 0.5 \cdot v_*(B)\}\]

\[v_*(B) = 1 + 0.5 \cdot v_*(A)\]

Try "stay" being optimal at A: \(v_*(A) = 2 + 0.5 \cdot v_*(A)\) ⇒ \(0.5 \cdot v_*(A) = 2\) ⇒ \(v_*(A) = 4\).

Then \(v_*(B) = 1 + 0.5 \times 4 = 3\).

Verify: "go" from A gives \(0 + 0.5 \times 3 = 1.5 < 4\). So "stay" is indeed better. Done!

\(v_*(A) = 4, \; v_*(B) = 3\). Optimal policy: always "stay" in A, always "back" in B.

11. Key Difference: Expectation vs Optimality

Bellman Expectation (\(v_\pi, q_\pi\))

Averages over the policy: \(\sum_a \pi(a|s) \ldots\)

Tells you how good a given policy is.

Linear system of equations — can solve exactly.

Used in: Policy Evaluation, TD learning.

Bellman Optimality (\(v_*, q_*\))

Maximizes over actions: \(\max_a \ldots\)

Tells you how good the best possible policy is.

Non-linear (because of max) — typically solved iteratively.

Used in: Value Iteration, Q-learning.

Exam tell — which equation?

If the question specifies a policy (e.g., "random policy", "\(\pi(a|s)=0.25\)") → use expectation equations (sum weighted by \(\pi\)).
If the question says "find optimal" or "Value Iteration" → use optimality equations (max over actions).

Common mistake

Students often write \(\max\) in a policy-evaluation question, or write \(\sum_a \pi(a|s)\) when the question asks for \(v_*\). Always check: are you evaluating a fixed policy or finding the best one?

Formula Cards — Memorize These Four

Card 1: Bellman Expectation for vπ

\[v_\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a|s) \sum_{s',r} p(s',r|s,a)\bigl[r + \gamma \, v_\pi(s')\bigr]\]
Mnemonic: "Policy-weighted average of [reward + discounted next-state value]"

Card 2: Bellman Expectation for qπ

\[q_\pi(s,a) = \sum_{s',r} p(s',r|s,a)\Bigl[r + \gamma \sum_{a'} \pi(a'|s') \, q_\pi(s',a')\Bigr]\]
Mnemonic: "Transition-average of [reward + discounted policy-average of next q]"

Card 3: Bellman Optimality for v*

\[v_*(s) = \max_a \sum_{s',r} p(s',r|s,a)\bigl[r + \gamma \, v_*(s')\bigr]\]
Mnemonic: "Best action's [reward + discounted next-state value]" — replace Σπ with max

Card 4: Bellman Optimality for q*

\[q_*(s,a) = \sum_{s',r} p(s',r|s,a)\Bigl[r + \gamma \max_{a'} q_*(s',a')\Bigr]\]
Mnemonic: "Transition-average of [reward + discounted best-next-action q]" — replace Σπ with max
