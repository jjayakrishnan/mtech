1. Lexical Semantics

Before we can represent word meaning computationally, we need to understand how meaning works linguistically.

Key Terminology

  • Lemma: The canonical dictionary form of a word ("run" is the lemma for "runs", "running", "ran")
  • Word Sense: A specific meaning of a word (bank: financial institution vs. river bank)
  • Polysemy: A single word having multiple related senses
  • Homonymy: A single word form with unrelated meanings (bat: animal vs. cricket bat)

Lexical Relations

RelationDefinitionExample
SynonymySame (or nearly same) meaningbig / large
AntonymyOpposite meaninghot / cold, long / short
HyponymyIS-A relation (more specific)dog IS-A animal
HypernymyIS-A-TYPE-OF (more general)animal is hypernym of dog
MeronymyPART-OF relationwheel is part of car
Similarity vs. Relatedness: "Car" and "bicycle" are similar (both vehicles). "Car" and "gasoline" are related (associated) but not similar. Similarity is a subset of relatedness.

2. The Distributional Hypothesis

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps."

-- J.R. Firth, 1957

Core idea: Words that occur in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.

If "dog" and "cat" both appear in contexts like "the ___ sat on the mat", "feed the ___", "pet the ___", then we infer they have similar meanings. This is the foundation of all distributional/vector semantics.

3. Vector Semantics

The distributional hypothesis gives us a method: represent each word as a vector (a point in high-dimensional space) based on its context patterns.

The Key Insight

Words with similar meanings will have similar vectors (nearby points in the space).

This turns the philosophical question "what does a word mean?" into a geometric question: "where does this word live in vector space?"

4. Sparse Vectors

Term-Document Matrix

Each row is a word, each column is a document. Cell (i,j) = count of word i in document j.

Example: Term-Document Matrix

Doc 1 (Romeo)Doc 2 (Hamlet)Doc 3 (Othello)
love825
death397
king1124
jealous1110

The vector for "love" is [8, 2, 5]. The vector for "death" is [3, 9, 7]. We can compare these vectors to see if meanings are similar.

Word-Word (Co-occurrence) Matrix

Both rows and columns are words. Cell (i,j) = how often word i appears in the context (window) of word j. This directly encodes the distributional hypothesis.

Example: Co-occurrence Matrix (window = 1)

Corpus: "the cat sat", "the dog sat", "the cat ran"

Notice "cat" = [2,0,0,1,1] and "dog" = [1,0,0,1,0] have similar patterns (both co-occur with "the" and "sat").

5. TF-IDF Weighting

Raw counts are noisy: common words like "the" dominate. TF-IDF downweights words that appear everywhere (low information) and upweights words distinctive to specific documents.

TF-IDF Formula

\[\text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \text{TF}(t, d) \times \text{IDF}(t)\]

\[\text{TF}(t, d) = \text{count of term } t \text{ in document } d\]

\[\text{IDF}(t) = \log_{10}\left(\frac{N}{df_t}\right)\]

where \(N\) = total number of documents, \(df_t\) = number of documents containing term \(t\).

Intuition:
  • TF (Term Frequency): A word that appears 10 times in a document is more important to that document than one appearing once.
  • IDF (Inverse Document Frequency): A word appearing in 1 out of 10,000 documents is more informative than one appearing in 9,000 out of 10,000. IDF("the") is tiny; IDF("astrophysics") is large.

Worked Example

Given: Corpus has N = 10,000 documents.

  • Term "neural" appears 5 times in document d, and in 50 documents total.
  • Term "the" appears 30 times in document d, and in 9,900 documents total.

For "neural" in document d:

\[\text{TF} = 5\]

\[\text{IDF} = \log_{10}\left(\frac{10000}{50}\right) = \log_{10}(200) \approx 2.301\]

\[\text{TF-IDF} = 5 \times 2.301 = 11.505\]

For "the" in document d:

\[\text{TF} = 30\]

\[\text{IDF} = \log_{10}\left(\frac{10000}{9900}\right) = \log_{10}(1.0101) \approx 0.0044\]

\[\text{TF-IDF} = 30 \times 0.0044 = 0.132\]

Despite "the" appearing 6x more often in this document, its TF-IDF is 87x smaller than "neural" because it provides almost no discriminating information.

6. Measuring Similarity: Cosine Similarity

Once words are vectors, we need a way to measure how similar two vectors are. We use cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between them.

Cosine Similarity

\[\cos(\vec{A}, \vec{B}) = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{|\vec{A}| \cdot |\vec{B}|} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i B_i}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i^2} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} B_i^2}}\]

Range: \([-1, 1]\) for general vectors; \([0, 1]\) for non-negative count vectors.

1 = identical direction, 0 = orthogonal (unrelated), -1 = opposite.

Why cosine and not Euclidean distance? Cosine is independent of vector magnitude. A long document and a short document about the same topic will have similar cosine but very different Euclidean distances (the long document has bigger counts everywhere). Cosine measures direction, which captures meaning.

Worked Example

Given: \(\vec{A} = [3, 2, 1]\) and \(\vec{B} = [1, 2, 3]\)

Step 1: Dot product

\[\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = (3)(1) + (2)(2) + (1)(3) = 3 + 4 + 3 = 10\]

Step 2: Magnitudes

\[|\vec{A}| = \sqrt{3^2 + 2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{9+4+1} = \sqrt{14} \approx 3.742\]

\[|\vec{B}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{1+4+9} = \sqrt{14} \approx 3.742\]

Step 3: Cosine

\[\cos(\vec{A}, \vec{B}) = \frac{10}{3.742 \times 3.742} = \frac{10}{14} \approx 0.714\]

These vectors are fairly similar (cosine = 0.714 out of max 1.0).

7. Problems with Sparse Vectors

  • High dimensionality: Vocabulary can be 50,000+ words, so each vector has 50,000+ dimensions. Most operations are expensive.
  • Sparsity: Most entries are zero (a word co-occurs with only a tiny fraction of the vocabulary). Wastes memory storing zeros.
  • No generalization: "car" and "automobile" may have completely different vectors if they never share the exact same contexts, even though they mean the same thing. Sparse vectors can't "infer" similarity.
  • Noisy: Low counts are unreliable estimates of association.

8. Dense Vectors (Preview)

The solution: learn short, dense vectors (typically 50-300 dimensions) where every dimension carries information. These are called word embeddings.

Key Properties of Dense Embeddings

  • Low-dimensional (100-300d vs. 50,000d)
  • Every dimension is a real number (no zeros)
  • Learned from data to capture meaning patterns
  • Can generalize: similar words get similar vectors even without identical contexts
  • Examples: Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText (covered in Lesson 4)

9. One-Hot Encoding vs. Dense Embeddings

PropertyOne-Hot EncodingDense Embedding

SimilarityAll pairs have cosine = 0 (orthogonal!)Similar words have high cosine

Critical insight for exams: One-hot vectors are the worst word representation because any two different words have cosine similarity of exactly 0. "King" is as different from "queen" as it is from "banana". Dense embeddings solve this.

Practice Questions

Question: A corpus has N = 1,000 documents. The word "transformer" appears 8 times in document D and appears in 20 documents total. The word "model" appears 12 times in document D and appears in 800 documents total.

Compute the TF-IDF for both words in document D. Which word is more informative for this document?

Solution:

For "transformer":

\[\text{TF} = 8, \quad \text{IDF} = \log_{10}\left(\frac{1000}{20}\right) = \log_{10}(50) \approx 1.699\]

\[\text{TF-IDF} = 8 \times 1.699 = 13.59\]

For "model":

\[\text{TF} = 12, \quad \text{IDF} = \log_{10}\left(\frac{1000}{800}\right) = \log_{10}(1.25) \approx 0.097\]

\[\text{TF-IDF} = 12 \times 0.097 = 1.16\]

"transformer" is far more informative (TF-IDF = 13.59 vs. 1.16) because it appears in few documents, making it a distinctive keyword.

Question: Compute the cosine similarity between these word vectors:

  • \(\vec{\text{king}} = [4, 3, 2, 1]\)
  • \(\vec{\text{queen}} = [3, 4, 2, 1]\)
  • \(\vec{\text{apple}} = [0, 1, 4, 3]\)

Compute cos(king, queen) and cos(king, apple). Which pair is more similar?

Solution:

cos(king, queen):

\[\text{Dot} = (4)(3)+(3)(4)+(2)(2)+(1)(1) = 12+12+4+1 = 29\]

\[|\text{king}| = \sqrt{16+9+4+1} = \sqrt{30} \approx 5.477\]

\[|\text{queen}| = \sqrt{9+16+4+1} = \sqrt{30} \approx 5.477\]

\[\cos = \frac{29}{5.477 \times 5.477} = \frac{29}{30} \approx 0.967\]

cos(king, apple):

\[\text{Dot} = (4)(0)+(3)(1)+(2)(4)+(1)(3) = 0+3+8+3 = 14\]

\[|\text{apple}| = \sqrt{0+1+16+9} = \sqrt{26} \approx 5.099\]

\[\cos = \frac{14}{5.477 \times 5.099} = \frac{14}{27.93} \approx 0.501\]

king-queen (0.967) is much more similar than king-apple (0.501), as expected!

Question: Identify the lexical relation for each pair:

  1. happy / joyful
  2. happy / sad
  3. dog / animal
  4. wheel / car
  5. bank (financial) / bank (river)

Solution:

  1. Synonymy (same meaning)
  2. Antonymy (opposite meaning)
  3. Hyponymy (dog IS-A animal; equivalently, animal is the hypernym of dog)
  4. Meronymy (wheel is PART-OF car)
  5. Homonymy (same form, unrelated meanings)

Question: Document A (1000 words) and Document B (100 words) are both about "machine learning." Their raw count vectors will be roughly proportional but very different in magnitude. Explain why cosine similarity handles this correctly but Euclidean distance does not.

Solution:

If Document A has counts [50, 30, 20, ...] and Document B has counts [5, 3, 2, ...], they point in the same direction in vector space (proportional counts), but Document A's vector is 10x longer.

Euclidean distance measures the straight-line gap between the tips of the vectors. Since A's tip is far from B's tip (due to magnitude difference), Euclidean says they are very different -- wrong!

Cosine similarity normalizes by magnitude and measures only the angle. Since A and B point in the same direction, cosine = 1.0 (maximally similar) -- correct!

In short: cosine captures what a document is about regardless of how long it is.
