3. Key Formula Cheat Sheet

Bigram MLE Probability
$$ P_{\text{MLE}}(w_n \mid w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1},\, w_n)}{C(w_{n-1})} $$
Laplace (Add-1) Smoothing
$$ P_{\text{Laplace}}(w_n \mid w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1},\, w_n) + 1}{C(w_{n-1}) + V} $$

V = vocabulary size

SGNS Loss (Negative Sampling)
$$ \mathcal{L} = -\log \sigma(\mathbf{v}_{w_t}^\top \mathbf{u}_{w_c}) - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \sigma(-\mathbf{v}_{w_t}^\top \mathbf{u}_{w_k}) $$

\(\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})\), K = number of negative samples

Cosine Similarity
$$ \text{sim}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\|\,\|\mathbf{b}\|} $$
TF-IDF
$$ \text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \text{tf}(t,d) \times \log\!\left(\frac{N}{df(t)}\right) $$

N = total docs, df(t) = docs containing term t

Softmax (Neural LM output)
$$ P(w_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} $$
Viterbi Recursion
$$ \delta_t(j) = \max_i \bigl[\delta_{t-1}(i) \cdot P(j \mid i)\bigr] \cdot P(w_t \mid j) $$

Init: \(\delta_1(j) = P(\text{START}{\to}j) \cdot P(w_1|j)\)

Forward Algorithm
$$ \alpha_t(j) = \left[\sum_i \alpha_{t-1}(i) \cdot P(j \mid i)\right] \cdot P(o_t \mid j) $$

Init: \(\alpha_1(j) = \pi_j \cdot P(o_1|j)\)

Attention Output (Contextual Embedding)
$$ \text{context}(w) = \sum_i \alpha_i \, \mathbf{v}_i $$

\(\alpha\) = attention weights, \(\mathbf{v}_i\) = value vectors

MMR (Maximal Marginal Relevance)
$$ \text{MMR}(s) = \lambda \cdot \text{sim}(s, q) - (1-\lambda) \cdot \max_{s_j \in S} \text{sim}(s, s_j) $$

\(\lambda\) balances relevance vs diversity

RAG Context Budget
$$ N_{\text{chunks}} = \left\lfloor \frac{L_{\text{ctx}} - T_{\text{query}} - T_{\text{sys}}}{T_{\text{chunk}}} \right\rfloor $$

L = context limit, T = token count

FFNN-LM Hidden Layer (ReLU)
$$ h = \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b) = \max\!\bigl(0,\; \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b\bigr) $$

(B) [3 Marks] FFNN-LM forward pass for P(learning | deep). Architecture: input x = [0.4, −0.2], hidden weights w = [0.5, −0.5], bias b = 0.1, ReLU activation. Output scores: z(learning) = 2h, z(thought) = 0.5h. Compute softmax P(learning).

$$ h_{\text{in}} = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b = (0.5)(0.4) + (-0.5)(-0.2) + 0.1 = 0.2 + 0.1 + 0.1 = 0.4 $$

Step 2: ReLU activation

$$ h = \text{ReLU}(0.4) = \max(0, 0.4) = 0.4 $$

Step 3: Output scores

z(learning) = 2 × 0.4 = 0.8 z(thought) = 0.5 × 0.4 = 0.2

Step 4: Softmax

$$ P(\text{learning}) = \frac{e^{0.8}}{e^{0.8} + e^{0.2}} = \frac{2.2255}{2.2255 + 1.2214} = \frac{2.2255}{3.4469} \approx 0.645 $$

P(learning | deep) via FFNN-LM ≈ 0.645

Note: The neural model (0.645) gives a higher probability than Laplace smoothing (0.25) because it learns distributional patterns from the embeddings, not just raw counts.

Question 4[6 Marks]
Consider the sentence "The cat sat". Word embeddings: \(\mathbf{x}_\text{the}=[1,0,1]\), \(\mathbf{x}_\text{cat}=[0,1,1]\), \(\mathbf{x}_\text{sat}=[1,1,0]\). Attention weights for "cat": \(\alpha = [0.2,\; 0.5,\; 0.3]\) (for the, cat, sat respectively). Value vectors: \(\mathbf{v}_\text{the}=[1,0]\), \(\mathbf{v}_\text{cat}=[1,1]\), \(\mathbf{v}_\text{sat}=[0,1]\).
(a) [1.5 Marks] Explain what contextual embeddings are and why they are important in NLP.
Show Answer — Part (a)

Contextual embeddings are word representations whose vector values change depending on the surrounding words in the sentence. Unlike static embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) that assign one fixed vector per word, contextual embeddings compute a different representation each time based on the full sentence context.

Why important: Natural language is highly ambiguous. The word "bank" in "river bank" has a completely different meaning from "bank account." A contextual embedding captures which sense is intended based on surrounding words — enabling models to handle polysemy, coreference, and syntactic disambiguation.

(b) [1.5 Marks] How do contextual embeddings differ from static embeddings like Word2Vec?
Show Answer — Part (b)
PropertyStatic (Word2Vec)Contextual (BERT, ELMo)
RepresentationOne vector per word typeDifferent vector per word token (depends on context)
PolysemyCannot handle — "bank" always gets the same vectorHandles naturally — each instance gets a context-aware vector
TrainingPredict surrounding words from a word (local context window)Predict masked tokens from the full sentence (transformer attention)
Downstream useFixed lookup tableFine-tuned per task or used as features

Show Answer — Part (c)

Question 6[6 Marks]
A Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system configuration:
  • Query tokens = 160
  • System prompt tokens = 240
  • Maximum LLM context length = 4096 tokens
  • Each retrieved document chunk = 480 tokens
(a) [2 Marks] Calculate the maximum number of document chunks that can be retrieved without exceeding the context limit.
Show Answer — Part (a)
$$ \text{Tokens available for chunks} = 4096 - 160 - 240 = 3696 $$ $$ N_{\text{chunks}} = \left\lfloor \frac{3696}{480} \right\rfloor = \lfloor 7.7 \rfloor = 7 $$

Maximum retrievable chunks = 7

(b) [2 Marks] If only 75% of the maximum chunks are actually retrieved, calculate the total number of tokens passed to the LLM.
Show Answer — Part (b)
$$ \text{Retrieved chunks} = \lfloor 0.75 \times 7 \rfloor = \lfloor 5.25 \rfloor = 5 $$ $$ \text{Total tokens} = 160 + 240 + (5 \times 480) = 400 + 2400 = 2800 $$

Total tokens passed to LLM = 2800

(c) [2 Marks] Justify whether retrieving fewer chunks (2800 tokens used vs 3736 at max) is likely to improve or degrade generation quality.
Show Answer — Part (c)

It could go either way — context is not the only factor:

  • Potential degradation: Fewer chunks = less retrieved context. If any of the 2 excluded chunks contained the specific fact needed to answer the query, the LLM will hallucinate or give an incomplete answer.
  • Potential improvement: "Lost in the middle" effect — LLMs often ignore information in the middle of long contexts. 5 high-quality, highly-relevant chunks may produce better answers than 7 chunks where 2 are tangential noise.
  • Key insight: Retrieval precision matters more than volume. If the retriever ranks documents well, 5 chunks may be sufficient.

In general: degradation is more likely if retrieval quality is high; improvement is possible if extra chunks add noise. The 1296 unused tokens represent a missed opportunity unless the excluded chunks are low-relevance.

(b) [1 Mark] Identify which sentence is selected next.
Show Answer — Part (b)

S1 is selected (MMR = 0.36 > 0.248).

(c) [2 Marks] Justify this selection logically — why does MMR prefer S1 despite S2 having higher query similarity?
Show Answer — Part (c)

MMR trades off relevance against redundancy:

  • S2 has higher raw relevance to the query (0.88 vs 0.80) — it talks about countries and AI medical diagnosis laws.
  • However, S2 is also very similar to the sentence already in the summary (sim=0.70) — it covers much of the same ground (AI medical diagnosis, laws).
  • S1 introduces new content: patient data privacy and ethical ML use. Its low redundancy (sim=0.30 with existing summary) means it adds more new information.
  • With λ=0.6, MMR weights relevance slightly higher than diversity but still penalises S2's high redundancy heavily enough that S1 wins.

MMR prioritises informational diversity: S1 is preferred because it adds more new knowledge to the summary, even though S2 is slightly more relevant to the query in isolation.

Question 2[4 Marks]
Corpus: "read a book", "read a blog". Test bigram: "read a map". Vocabulary V = {read, a, book, blog, map} = 5 words.
(a) MLE probability P(map | a). What problem arises for total sentence probability? [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (a)
$$ P_{\text{MLE}}(\text{map} \mid a) = \frac{C(a,\text{map})}{C(a)} = \frac{0}{2} = 0 $$

Problem: P(map|a)=0 → entire sentence probability = 0 (zero-product problem). Any unseen bigram in the test set makes the full sentence probability zero, regardless of how plausible the rest is.

(b) Laplace-smoothed P(map | a), V=5. [2 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (b)
$$ P_{\text{Laplace}}(\text{map} \mid a) = \frac{C(a,\text{map})+1}{C(a)+V} = \frac{0+1}{2+5} = \frac{1}{7} \approx 0.143 $$

Laplace smoothing gives every unseen bigram a small non-zero probability, avoiding the zero-product problem.

Question 3[5 Marks]
Skip-Gram with Negative Sampling. Target: "doctor", positive: "hospital", negatives: "car", "banana" (k=2). Vectors: doctor=[1,2,−1], hospital=[2,−1,1], car=[−1,1,0.5], banana=[0.5,−0.5,−1].
Compute total SGNS loss for this training example. [5 Marks]
Show Answer

SGNS loss: \( \mathcal{L} = -\log\sigma(v_c \cdot u_o) - \sum_{k=1}^{K}\log\sigma(-v_c \cdot u_k) \)

Dot products

$$ v_{\text{doctor}} \cdot u_{\text{hospital}} = (1)(2)+(2)(-1)+(-1)(1) = 2-2-1 = -1 $$ $$ v_{\text{doctor}} \cdot u_{\text{car}} = (1)(-1)+(2)(1)+(-1)(0.5) = -1+2-0.5 = 0.5 $$ $$ v_{\text{doctor}} \cdot u_{\text{banana}} = (1)(0.5)+(2)(-0.5)+(-1)(-1) = 0.5-1+1 = 0.5 $$

Sigmoid values: \(\sigma(x)=1/(1+e^{-x})\)

Question 6[4 Marks]
Sentences: "I will book the table." and "I read the book." The word "book" appears with different meanings.
Explain how POS tagging helps prevent mistranslation or mis-disambiguation of "book". [4 Marks]
Show Answer

Sentence 1: "I will book the table." — POS tagger assigns VB (verb) to "book". Meaning: to make a reservation.

Sentence 2: "I read the book." — POS tagger assigns NN (noun) to "book". Meaning: a physical book.

Why POS helps:

  • Disambiguation: Same surface form "book" maps to different word senses. POS tag is a strong feature for WSD systems to select the correct sense from a sense inventory (e.g., WordNet).
  • Translation: In machine translation, the target-language word for verb-"book" (réserver in French) is different from noun-"book" (livre). Without POS, the translator cannot distinguish them.
  • Downstream NLP: Dependency parsers, information extraction, and coreference resolution all rely on POS to determine grammatical roles, which cascades to correct semantic interpretation.

Question 4[1.5 + 1.5 + 3 = 6 Marks]
Sentence "The cat sat". \(\mathbf{x}_\text{the}=[1,0,1]\), \(\mathbf{x}_\text{cat}=[0,1,1]\), \(\mathbf{x}_\text{sat}=[1,1,0]\). Attention weights for "cat": \(\alpha=[0.2,0.5,0.3]\). Value vectors: \(\mathbf{v}_\text{the}=[1,0]\), \(\mathbf{v}_\text{cat}=[1,1]\), \(\mathbf{v}_\text{sat}=[0,1]\).
(a) What are contextual embeddings and why are they important? [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (a)

Contextual embeddings represent a word as a vector that depends on its surrounding context (the full sentence), not just the word itself. The same word gets different vectors in different contexts (e.g. "bank" in "river bank" vs "savings bank"). Important because language is inherently ambiguous — meaning is determined by context, not by isolated word identity.

(b) How do contextual embeddings differ from static Word2Vec? [1.5 Marks]
Show Answer — Part (b)

Static (Word2Vec): Each word has one fixed vector regardless of context. "bank" always maps to the same point in embedding space.

Contextual (ELMo, BERT): The representation is computed dynamically from the full input sequence using deep neural networks. "bank" in different sentences produces different vectors. Handles polysemy, syntax-sensitive semantics, and long-range dependencies.

$$ \text{MMR}(S1) = 0.6 \times 0.80 - 0.4 \times 0.30 = 0.48 - 0.12 = \mathbf{0.36} $$ $$ \text{MMR}(S2) = 0.6 \times 0.88 - 0.4 \times 0.70 = 0.528 - 0.28 = \mathbf{0.248} $$
