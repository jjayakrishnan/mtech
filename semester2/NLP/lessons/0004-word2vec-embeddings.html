1. Why Dense Vectors?

Traditional approaches like TF-IDF and one-hot encoding produce sparse vectors — mostly zeros, with no notion of similarity between words.

Analogy

Sparse vectors are like postal addresses: "42 Oak St" and "43 Oak St" are neighbours in reality, but as strings they share no systematic relationship. Dense embeddings are like GPS coordinates — nearby points in the space really are nearby in meaning.

Problems with sparse representations:

  • No generalization — "cat" and "kitten" are as far apart as "cat" and "democracy"
  • Extremely high-dimensional (|V| can be 50,000+)
  • No notion of semantic similarity baked in

Dense embeddings map each word to a short, real-valued vector (typically 100-300 dimensions) where geometric closeness encodes semantic relatedness.

Definition

A word embedding is a learned mapping \(f: V \to \mathbb{R}^d\) where \(d \ll |V|\), such that semantically similar words map to nearby points.

2. The Prediction-Based Approach

Instead of counting co-occurrences in a matrix (like SVD on term-document matrices), the prediction approach trains a classifier:

  1. Design a prediction task using raw text (no labels needed — self-supervised)
  2. Train a simple neural network to solve that task
  3. Extract the learned weight matrix as your embeddings
Key Insight

The embeddings are a byproduct of training. We do not care about the classifier's accuracy on the training task — we only want the internal representation it learned.

The core idea (Firth, 1957): "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." If two words appear in similar contexts, they must mean similar things. The prediction task forces the model to encode this distributional information into the vectors.

3. Word2Vec — Two Architectures

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

Given the surrounding context words, predict the center word.

Example: "The cat [???] on the mat" → predict "sat"

  • Input: context words (e.g., "The", "cat", "on", "the", "mat")
  • Operation: average their embeddings
  • Output: predict the center word via softmax

Skip-gram

Given the center word, predict the surrounding context words.

Example: given "sat" → predict "The", "cat", "on", "the", "mat"

  • Input: the center word
  • Output: predict each context word independently
  • Works better for rare words and smaller datasets
Plain English

CBOW = "fill in the blank" (given surroundings, guess the missing word).
Skip-gram = "predict the neighbours" (given a word, guess what's around it).

Exam Tip

Skip-gram is more commonly tested. Remember: Skip-gram predicts context FROM center. CBOW predicts center FROM context. The names are counter-intuitive — "skip-gram" skips the center to predict the gram (context).

4. The Training Objective

For Skip-gram, we want to maximize the probability of observing the actual context words given the center word:

\[\text{Maximize} \;\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{\substack{-c \leq j \leq c \\ j \neq 0}} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)\]

where \(T\) is the corpus length and \(c\) is the context window size.

The probability is computed using a softmax over the entire vocabulary:

\[P(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{u}_o \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(\mathbf{u}_w \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)}\]
Two sets of vectors

Each word gets two vectors: \(\mathbf{v}_w\) (when it is the center word) and \(\mathbf{u}_w\) (when it is a context word). After training, we typically use only \(\mathbf{v}_w\) or average both.

5. The Embedding Matrix

The learned parameters form a matrix \(\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{|V| \times d}\):

  • Each row corresponds to one word's embedding vector
  • Row \(i\) = the \(d\)-dimensional embedding for word \(i\)

Lookup operation: To get the embedding for word \(w_i\), multiply the one-hot vector by the embedding matrix:

\[\mathbf{e}_i = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{W}\]

where \(\mathbf{x}_i\) is the one-hot vector (all zeros except position \(i\)).

Plain English

Multiplying a one-hot vector by a matrix just "selects" the corresponding row. It is equivalent to a table lookup, but is differentiable (so gradients can flow through it during training).

Example: If \(|V| = 10{,}000\) and \(d = 300\), the embedding matrix has 3,000,000 parameters.

6. Negative Sampling

The full softmax denominator \(\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(\mathbf{u}_w \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)\) requires summing over the entire vocabulary (e.g., 100,000 words). This is computationally prohibitive.

Solution: Instead of computing the full softmax, reformulate as a binary classification problem:

  • Positive example: (center word, actual context word) → label = 1
  • Negative examples: (center word, \(k\) randomly sampled words) → label = 0

The objective becomes:

\[\log \sigma(\mathbf{u}_o \cdot \mathbf{v}_c) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathbb{E}_{w_i \sim P_n(w)} \left[\log \sigma(-\mathbf{u}_{w_i} \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)\right]\]

where \(\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}\) is the sigmoid function and \(P_n(w) \propto f(w)^{3/4}\) is the noise distribution (unigram raised to the 3/4 power).

Exam Tip

Typical values: \(k = 5\text{-}20\) for small datasets, \(k = 2\text{-}5\) for large datasets. The 3/4 power smooths the distribution so rare words get sampled more often than pure unigram frequency would suggest.

7. Properties of Learned Embeddings

Word Analogies

The famous result: vector arithmetic captures semantic relationships.

\[\mathbf{v}_{\text{king}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} + \mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}} \approx \mathbf{v}_{\text{queen}}\]

This works because the model learns that the "royalty" direction and the "gender" direction are separate linear subspaces.

Semantic Clusters

Words with similar meanings cluster together: {dog, cat, puppy, kitten} are nearby; {Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday} form another cluster.

8. GloVe: Global Vectors

GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) combines the best of count-based and prediction-based methods:

Core Idea

Learn word vectors such that their dot product equals the logarithm of co-occurrence count:
\(\mathbf{w}_i \cdot \mathbf{w}_j + b_i + b_j \approx \log(X_{ij})\)

GloVe Objective:

\[J = \sum_{i,j=1}^{|V|} f(X_{ij})\left(\mathbf{w}_i \cdot \mathbf{w}_j + b_i + b_j - \log X_{ij}\right)^2\]

where:

  • \(X_{ij}\) = number of times word \(j\) appears in the context of word \(i\)
  • \(f(X_{ij})\) = weighting function that caps very frequent co-occurrences: \(f(x) = \min\left((x/x_{\max})^{0.75},\; 1\right)\)
  • \(b_i, b_j\) = bias terms for each word
Why GloVe works

Word2Vec implicitly factorizes a shifted PMI matrix. GloVe makes this explicit: directly train vectors to reconstruct the log co-occurrence matrix, weighted so rare events do not dominate.

9. Word2Vec vs GloVe — Comparison

AspectWord2Vec (Skip-gram)GloVe
ApproachPrediction-based (local context)Count-based + prediction (global stats)
TrainingStochastic, window slides through corpusMatrix factorization on full co-occurrence
Data usedLocal context windowsGlobal co-occurrence matrix
ObjectiveMaximize P(context|center)Minimize weighted squared error on log counts
ScalabilityScales well to huge corporaNeeds full co-occurrence matrix in memory
PerformanceStrong on analogy tasksStrong on analogy + similarity tasks
Key paperMikolov et al. (2013)Pennington et al. (2014)
Exam Tip

If asked "what is the relationship between Word2Vec and count-based methods?" — the answer is that Levy & Goldberg (2014) showed Skip-gram with negative sampling implicitly factorizes a shifted PMI matrix. GloVe makes this explicit.

10. Limitations

  • One vector per word (no polysemy): "bank" (financial) and "bank" (river) get the same vector. The model averages all senses into one point.
  • Static embeddings: The vector for "bank" is the same regardless of whether it appears in "river bank" or "bank account". No context sensitivity.
  • Out-of-vocabulary (OOV): Cannot handle words not seen during training.
  • Subword information ignored: "unhappy" bears no systematic relation to "happy" unless both were common in training.
Why this matters

These limitations motivated contextual embeddings (ELMo, BERT) — covered in later modules. For the mid-sem, know that Word2Vec/GloVe are static (type-level), not contextual (token-level).

Formula Cards — Memorize for Exam

Card 1: Skip-gram Objective

\[\text{Maximize} \;\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{\substack{-c \leq j \leq c \\ j \neq 0}} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)\]
Mnemonic: "For each center word, maximize log-prob of its actual context words."

Card 2: Softmax Probability

\[P(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{u}_o \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(\mathbf{u}_w \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)}\]
Numerator: dot product of context-vector of output word with center-vector of input word. Denominator: sum over ALL words.

Card 3: Negative Sampling Objective

\[\log \sigma(\mathbf{u}_o \cdot \mathbf{v}_c) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathbb{E}_{w_i \sim P_n} \left[\log \sigma(-\mathbf{u}_{w_i} \cdot \mathbf{v}_c)\right]\]
Push true context closer (positive dot product), push random words away (negative dot product). k = number of negatives.

Card 4: GloVe Objective

\[J = \sum_{i,j=1}^{|V|} f(X_{ij})\left(\mathbf{w}_i \cdot \mathbf{w}_j + b_i + b_j - \log X_{ij}\right)^2\]
Weighted least-squares: make dot product of vectors approximate log co-occurrence count. f(X) caps frequent pairs.

Card 5: Cosine Similarity

\[\text{cos}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{||\mathbf{a}|| \; ||\mathbf{b}||} = \frac{\sum_i a_i b_i}{\sqrt{\sum_i a_i^2}\;\sqrt{\sum_i b_i^2}}\]
Range: [-1, 1]. Used to measure word similarity from embeddings.

Try It Yourself

Exercise 1: Cosine Similarity Computation

Given these 3-dimensional word vectors:

  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{cat}} = [0.7, \; 0.5, \; 0.1]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{dog}} = [0.6, \; 0.6, \; 0.2]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{car}} = [0.1, \; 0.2, \; 0.9]\)

Compute: (a) cos(cat, dog)   (b) cos(cat, car)   (c) Which pair is more similar?

Solution:

(a) \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{cat}} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{dog}} = 0.7 \times 0.6 + 0.5 \times 0.6 + 0.1 \times 0.2 = 0.42 + 0.30 + 0.02 = 0.74\)

\(||\mathbf{v}_{\text{cat}}|| = \sqrt{0.49 + 0.25 + 0.01} = \sqrt{0.75} \approx 0.866\)

\(||\mathbf{v}_{\text{dog}}|| = \sqrt{0.36 + 0.36 + 0.04} = \sqrt{0.76} \approx 0.872\)

\(\text{cos(cat, dog)} = \frac{0.74}{0.866 \times 0.872} = \frac{0.74}{0.755} \approx 0.98\)

(b) \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{cat}} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{car}} = 0.07 + 0.10 + 0.09 = 0.26\)

\(||\mathbf{v}_{\text{car}}|| = \sqrt{0.01 + 0.04 + 0.81} = \sqrt{0.86} \approx 0.927\)

\(\text{cos(cat, car)} = \frac{0.26}{0.866 \times 0.927} = \frac{0.26}{0.803} \approx 0.32\)

(c) cat and dog are much more similar (0.98 vs 0.32), which matches our intuition.

Exercise 2: Word Analogy

Given vectors (simplified 2D):

  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{king}} = [4.0, \; 2.0]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} = [2.0, \; 2.0]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}} = [2.0, \; 4.0]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{queen}} = [4.0, \; 4.0]\)
  • \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{prince}} = [3.5, \; 2.0]\)

Compute: king - man + woman = ? Which word is closest?

Solution:

\(\mathbf{v}_{\text{king}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} + \mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}} = [4-2+2, \; 2-2+4] = [4.0, \; 4.0]\)

This exactly matches \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{queen}} = [4.0, \; 4.0]\).

The "gender direction" is \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} = [0, 2]\). Adding this to king shifts it from "male royalty" to "female royalty" = queen.

Exercise 3: Softmax Computation

In a tiny vocabulary {cat, dog, fish}, the dot products with a center word give scores: \([2.0, \; 1.0, \; 0.5]\). Compute the softmax probability for each word.

Solution:

\(e^{2.0} = 7.389, \;\; e^{1.0} = 2.718, \;\; e^{0.5} = 1.649\)

Sum = \(7.389 + 2.718 + 1.649 = 11.756\)

\(P(\text{cat}) = 7.389 / 11.756 = 0.629\)

\(P(\text{dog}) = 2.718 / 11.756 = 0.231\)

\(P(\text{fish}) = 1.649 / 11.756 = 0.140\)

Check: \(0.629 + 0.231 + 0.140 = 1.000\) (valid probability distribution)

Sources: Mikolov et al. (2013) "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space"; Pennington et al. (2014) "GloVe: Global Vectors for Word Representation"; Jurafsky & Martin, Speech and Language Processing, Ch. 6.
